# Exit Readiness Audit

A structured audit of how prepared a business is for a potential exit, across five dimensions, with issues prioritised by transaction impact and remediation effort. Designed to be run annually regardless of whether an exit is being actively considered.

Exit readiness is almost universally treated as something to prepare for six to twelve months before a transaction. That is too late. The issues most damaging to a deal, customer concentration that was never diversified, founder dependency that was never documented away from a single person, IP that was never properly assigned, commercial agreements with problematic change-of-control clauses, and financial reporting that reflects a founder-managed company rather than an acquirable asset, require years rather than months to address properly. By the time a founder discovers them in a transaction process, they either kill the deal, force a price reduction, or are fixed under time pressure in ways that create new problems.

The same factors that make a business exit-ready also make it more resilient, more financeable, and easier to scale. Exit readiness is not preparation for a transaction. It is good business architecture.

---

## Who it is for

Founders at any stage who want to understand what an acquirer, investor, or financial buyer would find if they examined the business today, and to prioritise the remediation of issues that would impair a transaction or a valuation. Most valuable run annually, and again before engaging with any advisor on a transaction.

---

## The eight stages

**1. Business Context Capture**
Collects the raw material: what the business does and how it generates revenue, the current revenue run rate and growth trajectory, the ownership structure, any prior transaction activity, the founder's current thinking about exit, and their own assessment of the business's strengths and vulnerabilities from a buyer's perspective.

**2. First Principles Decomposition**
Establishes what an acquirer of this business is fundamentally buying, stripped of the revenue and the product: is it the customer relationships and recurring revenue, the technology and IP, the team and their expertise, or the market position? Then asks how dependent each of those assets is on the founder personally. Founder dependency is the single most common and most significant exit readiness problem, and identifying it before the audit begins determines where the audit should concentrate.

**3. Five-Dimension Audit**
The core of the review. Audits exit readiness across five dimensions, identifying specific issues within each, classifying each by severity, and noting the approximate remediation effort required.

Financial hygiene and reporting quality: whether the business's financial statements accurately reflect its performance and are prepared to a standard that supports a transaction. Covers management account quality, revenue recognition, expense intermingling, related-party transactions, capitalisation policies, financial model quality, and historical record completeness.

Legal and IP cleanliness: whether the business owns what it thinks it owns and whether there are legal liabilities that would transfer with the business. Covers IP assignment from employees and contractors, founder IP pre-incorporation, open source licence obligations, customer and supplier change-of-control provisions, unresolved disputes, and employment agreement adequacy.

Customer and revenue concentration: whether the revenue is distributed or concentrated, and whether the contracts supporting it are durable. Covers single-customer concentration thresholds, top-customer concentration, contract term and renewal provisions, churn visibility, and revenue dependency on founder relationships.

Operational dependency on key individuals: whether the business can operate and grow without its current founders and key employees. Covers revenue dependency on founder relationships, technical knowledge concentration, undocumented processes, key role succession, and founder involvement in operational decisions.

Strategic narrative coherence from a buyer's perspective: whether the business has a clear, credible, and differentiated strategic position that is supported by the evidence. Covers market size claim supportability, competitive differentiation credibility, growth narrative consistency with historical trajectory, target market definition, and management team composition.

**4. Bias Assessment**
Assesses the cognitive biases most likely to be distorting the founder's assessment of their own exit readiness. Runs a default scan of five biases: optimism bias, overconfidence, sunk cost influence, availability bias, and normalcy bias. Expands contextually to authority bias and narrative bias. Normalcy bias receives particular attention: issues that have existed for a long time without causing a visible problem tend to be assessed as less significant than they are because their persistence without consequence has made them feel normal.

**5. Prioritisation**
Prioritises every issue across two dimensions: transaction impact (deal-breaking, price-reducing, or manageable) and remediation effort (high, medium, or low with an approximate timeframe). The highest-priority issues are those classified as deal-breaking or price-reducing with medium or low remediation effort. Issues that are deal-breaking with high remediation effort go on a long-term plan with a realistic timeline.

**6. The Reconstruction**
For each deal-breaking or price-reducing issue, produces a specific remediation recommendation: what the issue is, what it would cost in a transaction, what the remediation requires, who needs to be involved, and the realistic timeline. Specific enough that a professional advisor could act on it without further clarification.

**7. The Summary**
Presented before the full staged analysis. Uses first principles thinking to return to what the business is actually selling to a buyer and state clearly whether the current state supports or undermines that value proposition. Three sentences: what an acquirer is fundamentally buying, the single most significant issue identified, and the recommended immediate action.

**8. Framing for Others**
Helps the founder communicate the audit findings to the relevant audience, drafted rather than described. Applies Toulmin's argumentation structure throughout. Three audience frames: board or investors, legal and financial advisors, and personal record for annual tracking of which issues have been addressed, which are in progress, and which have been consciously deferred.

## As a standalone framework

The five-dimension structure in Stage 3 serves as a due diligence preparation checklist for any founder approaching a transaction. Work through each dimension, identify the issues present, and classify them using the two-dimension prioritisation in Stage 5.

The first principles question in Stage 2 is the most useful standalone question in the skill: what is an acquirer of this business fundamentally buying, and how dependent is that asset on the founder personally? Answering that question honestly shapes every other remediation decision.

The distinction in Stage 5 between deal-breaking, price-reducing, and manageable issues is a useful classification system for any conversation with a legal or financial advisor about transaction readiness. It focuses the conversation on what matters most rather than on a comprehensive review of everything that is imperfect.
