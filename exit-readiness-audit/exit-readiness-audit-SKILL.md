# Exit Readiness Audit

This skill exists to help founders understand what an acquirer, investor, or financial buyer would find if they examined the business today, and to prioritise the remediation of the issues that would most impair a transaction or a valuation.

Exit readiness is almost universally treated as something to prepare for six to twelve months before a transaction. That is too late. The issues most damaging to a transaction, customer concentration, founder dependency, IP assignment gaps, problematic change-of-control clauses, and financial reporting that reflects a founder-managed company rather than an acquirable asset, require years rather than months to address properly. By the time a founder is in a transaction process and discovers them, they either kill the deal, force a price reduction, or are fixed under time pressure in ways that create new problems.

The same factors that make a business exit-ready also make it more resilient, more financeable, and easier to scale. Exit readiness is not preparation for a transaction. It is good business architecture.

Run this audit annually regardless of whether an exit is being actively considered. Run it again before engaging with any advisor on a transaction.

Work through each stage in order. Every stage produces output before the next begins.

---

## Opening Orientation

Before beginning, establish the following in plain language:

This process examines the business as an acquirer's due diligence process would examine it: systematically, across multiple dimensions, looking for issues that would impair a transaction, reduce a valuation, or introduce risk that a buyer would need to price in or walk away from.

The output is not a transaction readiness score. It is a prioritised list of issues classified by severity and remediation effort, with specific recommendations for each. The goal is to give the founder a clear view of what exists today and what needs to change, with enough time to address it before a transaction requires it.

---

## Stage 1: Business Context Capture

Collect the raw material before any analysis begins. Ask the founder to describe:

- What the business does and how it generates revenue
- The current revenue run rate and growth trajectory
- The ownership structure: founders, investors, employee option pool
- Whether there has been any prior transaction activity: previous fundraises, M&A conversations, secondary sales
- The founder's current thinking about exit: whether it is being actively considered, a long-term goal, or simply something they want to be prepared for
- What the founder believes the business's most significant strengths are from a buyer's perspective
- What the founder believes the business's most significant vulnerabilities are

The founder's own assessment of strengths and vulnerabilities is a useful starting point and will be tested against the structured audit.

---

## Stage 2: First Principles Decomposition

Before auditing the five dimensions, establish what an acquirer of this business would fundamentally be buying.

Strip away the revenue and the product and ask: what is the actual asset? In some businesses, the asset is the customer relationships and the recurring revenue they generate. In others, it is the technology and the IP. In others, it is the team and their expertise. In others, it is the market position and the defensibility of that position. In most businesses, it is some combination.

Then ask: how dependent is each of those assets on the founder personally? An acquirer buying a business that only functions because of the founder's relationships, judgment, or presence is buying a much riskier asset than one where the value is institutionalised. Founder dependency is the single most common and most significant exit readiness problem, and understanding what the business is actually selling before auditing how well it is packaged determines what the audit should concentrate on.

---

## Stage 3: Five-Dimension Audit

Audit the business's exit readiness across five dimensions. For each dimension, identify the specific issues present, classify each by severity, and note the approximate remediation effort required.

**Dimension 1: Financial hygiene and reporting quality**

An acquirer's financial due diligence will examine whether the business's financial statements accurately reflect its performance and are prepared to a standard that supports a transaction. Common issues include:

- Management accounts that are not prepared monthly or that are prepared inconsistently
- Revenue recognition practices that differ from what a buyer's auditors will require
- Intermingling of personal and business expenses
- Related-party transactions that are not documented at arm's length
- Capitalisation and amortisation policies that are not consistently applied
- Financial projections that are not grounded in a documented model with stated assumptions
- Incomplete or inconsistent records for historical periods a buyer will examine

Severity: issues that would cause an auditor to qualify accounts or that would require a restatement are critical. Issues that require adjustment but do not affect the fundamental picture are significant. Cosmetic presentation issues are peripheral.

**Dimension 2: Legal and IP cleanliness**

An acquirer will examine whether the business owns what it thinks it owns and whether there are legal liabilities that would transfer with the business. Common issues include:

- IP developed by employees or contractors that was not properly assigned to the company at the time it was created
- Founder IP that was developed before the company was incorporated and has not been formally assigned
- Open source components used in the product that carry licence obligations incompatible with a transaction
- Customer contracts that require customer consent to a change of control
- Supplier or partner agreements with change-of-control provisions that would give the counterparty the right to terminate
- Unresolved disputes, threatened or actual litigation, or regulatory issues
- Employment agreements that do not contain adequate IP assignment, non-compete, or non-solicitation clauses

Severity: IP ownership gaps are critical because they go to the heart of what the buyer is acquiring. Change-of-control provisions in material contracts are critical if they affect a significant proportion of revenue. Unresolved disputes are significant if they represent contingent liabilities that a buyer would need to price.

**Dimension 3: Customer and revenue concentration**

An acquirer will examine whether the revenue is distributed across a customer base or concentrated in a small number of customers, and whether the contracts supporting that revenue are durable. Common issues include:

- A single customer representing more than ten to fifteen percent of revenue
- The top three customers representing more than forty percent of revenue
- Revenue that is not contracted or that is contracted on short terms without renewal provisions
- High customer churn that is not visible in the topline revenue figures because it is masked by new customer acquisition
- Revenue that is dependent on a relationship between the customer and the founder personally rather than on a commercial agreement with the business

Severity: concentration issues are classified by the proportion of revenue affected and the contractual durability of those relationships. A single customer at thirty percent of revenue on a short-term contract with a change-of-control clause is a critical issue. The same customer on a long-term contracted relationship without change-of-control provisions is significant but manageable.

**Dimension 4: Operational dependency on key individuals**

An acquirer will examine whether the business can operate and grow without its current founders and key employees. Common issues include:

- Revenue that is dependent on the founder's personal relationships with customers
- Technical knowledge that exists only in one person's head and is not documented
- Processes that are not written down and that would be difficult to replicate if the person who runs them left
- Key roles with no succession plan and no deputy or documented handover material
- An organisational structure where too many things require founder sign-off or founder involvement to move forward

Severity: founder dependency on revenue-generating relationships is critical if it represents a material proportion of revenue and cannot be transferred to a commercial relationship with the business. Technical knowledge concentration is critical if it affects the buyer's ability to maintain or develop the product post-acquisition. Process dependency is significant and remediated through documentation.

**Dimension 5: Strategic narrative coherence from a buyer's perspective**

An acquirer will examine whether the business has a clear, credible, and differentiated strategic position and whether the narrative the founder tells about the business is supported by the evidence. Common issues include:

- A market size claim that is not supportable with credible third-party data
- A competitive differentiation claim that is not reflected in the product or the customer's stated reason for choosing the business
- A growth narrative that does not match the historical growth trajectory
- A target market that is too broad to be credible or too narrow to support the valuation being sought
- A management team composition that does not match the stage and ambition of the business

Severity: strategic narrative issues are significant because they affect the buyer's confidence in the investment thesis, but they are rarely critical in isolation. They become critical when they are combined with financial or legal issues, because they undermine the buyer's ability to diligence the business confidently.

---

## Stage 4: Bias Assessment

Assess the cognitive biases most likely to be distorting the founder's assessment of their own exit readiness.

**Default scan**

*Optimism bias:* whether the founder is assessing issues as less severe than an acquirer's due diligence team would assess them.

*Overconfidence:* whether the founder's confidence in the business's narrative and competitive position exceeds what the evidence would support under scrutiny.

*Sunk cost influence:* whether prior investment in a particular structure, relationship, or approach is making the founder reluctant to acknowledge that it represents an issue that needs to be addressed.

*Availability bias:* whether the founder's assessment of what a buyer cares about is shaped by what they have heard from advisors or investors rather than by what due diligence processes actually examine.

*Normalcy bias:* whether issues that have existed for a long time without causing a visible problem are being assessed as less significant than they are, because their persistence without consequence has made them feel normal.

**Contextual expansion**

*Authority bias:* whether the founder is deferring to the assessment of a lawyer, accountant, or advisor on a particular issue rather than independently verifying that the issue has been properly addressed.

*Narrative bias:* whether the founder's founding story and their own perception of the business's value are making it difficult to assess how a buyer who does not share that narrative would see the business.

For each bias assessed as present, identify which dimension of the audit it is most likely distorting and what the corrective adjustment is.

---

## Stage 5: Prioritisation

Prioritise every issue identified in Stage 3 across two dimensions.

**Transaction impact:** how much would this issue impair a transaction if discovered in due diligence? Classify as: deal-breaking (would cause a buyer to walk away or require fundamental restructuring of the deal), price-reducing (would result in a material reduction in valuation or an escrow or indemnity requirement), or manageable (would require disclosure and explanation but would not materially affect the transaction).

**Remediation effort:** how much time and resource would addressing this issue require? Classify as high, medium, or low, with an approximate timeframe for resolution.

The highest-priority issues are those classified as deal-breaking or price-reducing with medium or low remediation effort. These are the issues where remediation investment produces the highest return. Issues that are deal-breaking with high remediation effort should be on a long-term remediation plan with a realistic timeline.

Issues classified as manageable can be addressed closer to a transaction if necessary, but should not be ignored: manageable issues that are discovered in due diligence accumulate in the buyer's risk assessment even when each one individually is not material.

---

## Stage 6: The Reconstruction

For each issue classified as deal-breaking or price-reducing, produce a specific remediation recommendation: what the issue is, what it would cost in a transaction, what the remediation requires, who needs to be involved, and what the realistic timeline is.

The recommendations should be specific enough to act on immediately. "Address IP assignment gaps" is not a recommendation. "Commission a review of all employment contracts and contractor agreements entered into since incorporation to identify any where IP assignment clauses are absent or inadequate, and execute assignment agreements with all relevant parties" is a recommendation.

For each recommendation, establish:

- What needs to happen
- Who is responsible
- What the timeline is
- What the success indicator is: how the founder will know this issue has been resolved to a standard that would satisfy a buyer's due diligence

---

## Stage 7: The Summary

Present this before the full staged analysis.

Use first principles thinking as the underlying architecture. Return to what the business is actually selling to a buyer, identified in Stage 2, and state clearly whether the current state of the business supports or undermines that value proposition from a buyer's perspective.

Structure the summary in three parts:

First, state what an acquirer of this business is fundamentally buying, in one plain sentence.

Second, state the single most significant issue the audit has identified: the one that would most impair a transaction or a valuation if it were discovered in due diligence today.

Third, state the recommended immediate action in one sentence: the one thing the founder should address first.

The summary should be three sentences. The founder should understand the shape of the audit before entering the detail.

---

## Stage 8: Framing for Others

Help the founder communicate the audit findings to the relevant audience. The output is drafted, not described. Apply Toulmin's argumentation structure throughout: every claim accompanied by its grounds, the warrant connecting them, and acknowledgment of the conditions under which the claim would not hold.

**Board or investors**
The emphasis is on the current state of exit readiness, the highest-priority issues, the remediation plan and timeline, and how exit readiness improvement maps to broader business quality improvement. Boards and investors are more confident in founders who treat exit readiness as an ongoing business quality standard rather than a transaction-specific preparation exercise.

**Legal and financial advisors**
Where specific issues require professional remediation, the communication to advisors should be specific about what has been identified, what outcome is required, and what the timeline constraint is. Advisors who receive a clear brief produce better work than those who are asked to look at something generally.

**Personal record**
Document the full audit findings, prioritisation, and remediation recommendations for annual review. Track which issues have been addressed, which are in progress, and which have been consciously deferred and why. The annual comparison shows how exit readiness is evolving over time and ensures that deferred issues do not become forgotten ones.

---

## Communication Principles

**Honesty as the primary requirement**
The exit readiness audit has no value if issues are minimised or rationalised. An acquirer's due diligence team will not be charitable. The audit should apply the same standard.

**Specificity over generality**
Every issue should be described specifically enough that a professional advisor could act on it without further clarification. "Some IP may not be properly assigned" is not a specific finding. "Four contractor agreements from 2021 do not contain IP assignment clauses" is.

**Observation separated from judgment**
The audit examines the business's current state, not the quality of the founder's past decisions. Most exit readiness issues were created by reasonable decisions made without the full context of what a transaction would require. The audit is forward-facing.

**Progressive disclosure**
Lead with the deal-breaking and price-reducing issues. Make the full audit available but do not lead with a comprehensive list of every imperfection. A founder who leaves the audit overwhelmed by the volume of issues is less likely to act than one who leaves with a clear first priority.
