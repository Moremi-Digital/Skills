# Market Signal Review

A structured assessment that determines whether a specific piece of market feedback, customer input, competitive movement, or data point warrants a strategic response, a monitoring brief, or active discounting.

Founders are not short of information. The problem is that the sources most likely to generate noise, vocal customers, strong-opinioned investors, advisors applying pattern-matched but misaligned experience, are often the ones that receive the most weight. Meanwhile, weaker but more systematic signals are underweighted or missed entirely. The result is strategic decisions driven by the loudest voice in the room rather than the most evidentially grounded interpretation of what the market is actually doing.

This skill takes one signal at a time and works through a structured assessment of what it actually means, who it is coming from and why, whether it is corroborated by independent sources, what cognitive biases are distorting its interpretation, and what decision it would change if taken seriously. The output is a clear recommendation with explicit reasoning.

---

## Who it is for

Founders and senior leaders trying to interpret a specific piece of information that feels significant but whose meaning is not yet clear. This includes customer feedback that keeps recurring, a competitor's unexpected move, a data anomaly in the business's own metrics, a market trend that may or may not be relevant, or an investor or advisor comment that has lodged in the founder's thinking.

---

## The nine stages

**1. Signal Capture**
Collects the raw material: what the signal is exactly, when it was received and through what channel, who or what the source is, why it feels significant enough to examine, and what the founder's current instinct about it is before any analysis begins. The founder's instinct is made explicit at the outset so the review can engage with it rather than having it operate invisibly.

**2. First Principles Decomposition**
Before assessing the signal, establishes what it would need to mean for it to be strategically significant by stripping it back to the underlying condition it might be pointing to. Identifies two or three candidate interpretations before assessing which is most supported by the evidence. This prevents the review from confirming the first interpretation that comes to mind.

**3. Source Assessment**
Assesses the source across four dimensions: representativeness (does this source represent a pattern or an edge case), incentives and limitations (what shapes what this source reports), access and perspective (what they directly observed versus what they are inferring), and track record (how reliable this source has been previously where that is known).

**4. Corroboration Assessment**
Assesses whether the signal is corroborated by independent sources. A signal from one source through one channel is a data point. A signal that appears independently across multiple channels and sources is a pattern. Maps the corroboration explicitly and treats the absence of corroboration from sources where it would be expected as informative in its own right.

**5. Bias Assessment**
Assesses the cognitive biases most likely to be distorting the founder's interpretation. Runs a default scan of six biases: availability bias, confirmation bias, authority bias, overconfidence, anchoring, and negativity bias. Expands contextually to include recency bias and optimism or pessimism bias where relevant. Each bias assessed as present is mapped to what it is doing to the signal's interpreted weight and in which direction.

**6. Decision Implication Assessment**
Establishes what decision the signal would actually change if taken seriously. A signal that would not change any decision is noise by definition. Assesses the asymmetry between the cost of acting on the signal if it turns out to be noise and the cost of not acting if it turns out to be real. This asymmetry determines the appropriate response threshold.

**7. The Recommendation**
Produces a clear recommendation in one of three categories: strategic response (the signal warrants an immediate response and specifies what it is), monitoring brief (the signal is plausible but not yet sufficiently corroborated, and specifies what additional information would upgrade it and over what timeframe), or active discounting (the signal does not warrant strategic attention now, with the reasoning documented for future reference).

**8. The Summary**
Presented before the full staged analysis. Uses first principles thinking to return to the candidate interpretations from Stage 2 and state which one the evidence most supports. Three sentences: what the signal most likely means, the single most important factor in that assessment, and the recommendation category with the specific action or monitoring brief.

**9. Framing for Others**
Helps the founder communicate the signal assessment where appropriate. Applies Toulmin's argumentation structure throughout. Three audience frames: leadership team, board or investors, and personal record. Documenting signals that turn out to be noise is as valuable as documenting those that prompt response, because the pattern of what is signal versus noise in this specific market is itself strategic information.

## How to use it with Claude

1. Open [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai)
2. Start a new conversation
3. Upload `SKILL.md` from this folder using the attachment icon
4. Tell Claude what the signal is and where it came from. Claude will work through the assessment with you, asking for what it needs at each step.

## As a standalone framework

The three-category recommendation in Stage 7, strategic response, monitoring brief, or active discounting, is a useful decision rule for any signal assessment without Claude. Before deciding how to respond to any piece of market information, ask: is this corroborated, what decision would it change, and what is the asymmetry between acting on noise and missing a real signal? Those three questions will resolve most cases.

The source assessment questions in Stage 3 apply to any information source: what incentives shape what they report, what do they have direct visibility of versus what are they inferring, and how reliable have they been previously? Running these questions on a source before weighting their input reduces the influence of authority and recency on strategic decisions.
