# Market Signal Review

This skill exists to help founders and senior leaders distinguish signal from noise in the information environment around their business. The problem is not insufficient information. It is that the sources most likely to generate noise, vocal customers, strong-opinioned investors, advisors with pattern-matched but misapplied experience, are often the ones that receive the most weight, while weaker but more systematic signals are underweighted or missed.

This skill takes a specific piece of feedback or market information and works through a structured assessment of what it actually means, who it is coming from and why, whether it is corroborated, and what decision it would change if taken seriously. The output is a clear recommendation: strategic response, monitoring brief, or active discounting, with the reasoning made explicit.

Work through each stage in order. Every stage produces output before the next begins.

---

## Opening Orientation

Before beginning, establish the following with the founder in plain language:

This process examines one signal at a time. It does not evaluate the general state of the market or produce a broad strategic review. Its value is in the precision of the assessment: by examining a specific signal rigorously, it builds the founder's capacity to apply the same logic to future signals independently.

The output is a recommendation, not a decision. The founder retains the decision. The skill provides the structured reasoning to inform it.

---

## Stage 1: Signal Capture

Collect the raw material before any analysis begins. Ask the founder to describe:

- What the signal is, as precisely as possible: the exact feedback, data point, observation, or competitive movement
- When it was received and through what channel
- Who or what the source is
- Why this signal is being examined now: what has made it feel significant enough to warrant structured attention
- What the founder's current instinct about the signal is, before any analysis

That last point matters. The founder's instinct is data. It may reflect pattern recognition from experience that should be taken seriously, or it may reflect a bias that the analysis should surface. Either way, making it explicit before the analysis begins allows the review to engage with it rather than having it operate invisibly throughout.

---

## Stage 2: First Principles Decomposition

Before assessing the signal, establish what it would need to mean for it to be strategically significant. Strip away the specific form of the signal and ask: what is the underlying condition this signal might be pointing to?

A customer complaint about product speed might point to a product architecture problem, a customer expectation mismatch, a competitive shift, or a support process failure. Each has different strategic implications. A competitor's pricing change might point to their unit economics improving, their strategic positioning shifting, a capital injection, or a response to their own customer feedback. Each reads differently.

Identify two or three candidate interpretations of the signal before assessing which is most supported by the evidence. This prevents the review from confirming the first interpretation that comes to mind.

---

## Stage 3: Source Assessment

Assess the source of the signal across four dimensions.

**Representativeness:** does this source represent a pattern or an edge case? A single vocal customer may represent a segment or may be an outlier. A competitor's move may reflect a market-wide shift or their specific situation. Establish what the source can and cannot be taken to represent.

**Incentives and limitations:** what incentives or constraints shape what this source reports? A customer asking for a feature has an incentive to present their need as urgent and common. An investor sharing market intelligence has their own portfolio context that shapes what they notice and emphasise. An advisor pattern-matching from prior experience may be applying a model that does not fit this situation. None of these make the signal invalid, but they affect how much weight it should carry.

**Access and perspective:** what does this source have direct visibility of, and what are they inferring? A customer can report their own experience with precision. They cannot reliably report what other customers experience. A competitor's public actions are observable. Their internal reasoning is not. Distinguish between what the source directly observed and what they are interpreting.

**Track record:** where available, how reliable has this source been in the past? Has this customer's feedback previously correlated with broader patterns? Has this advisor's pattern-matching applied well to this business before?

---

## Stage 4: Corroboration Assessment

Assess whether the signal is corroborated by independent sources.

A signal that appears in one channel from one source is a data point. A signal that appears independently across multiple channels and sources is a pattern. The distinction is critical for determining the appropriate response.

Ask:

- Is there any other source, independent of the first, that is pointing to the same underlying condition?
- Is there data in the business's own metrics that is consistent with what the signal suggests?
- Is there public information, market data, or competitor behaviour that corroborates the interpretation?
- Is the absence of corroboration informative? If this signal were pointing to a real pattern, would you expect to see it elsewhere, and if you do not, what does that tell you?

Map the corroboration explicitly. Strong corroboration across independent sources increases the signal's weight significantly. Absence of corroboration from sources where it would be expected reduces it.

---

## Stage 5: Bias Assessment

Assess the cognitive biases most likely to be distorting the founder's interpretation of the signal.

**Default scan**

*Availability bias:* whether a recent experience, a previous success or failure with a similar signal, a vivid conversation, is making this signal feel more significant than its evidential weight warrants.

*Confirmation bias:* whether the signal is receiving weight because it confirms something the founder already believes or wants to believe about the market, the product, or the competitive situation.

*Authority bias:* whether the signal is being treated as significant primarily because of who delivered it rather than because of what it says.

*Overconfidence:* whether the founder's interpretation of the signal is more certain than the evidence supports.

*Anchoring:* whether an early interpretation of the signal is preventing the founder from genuinely considering alternative readings.

*Negativity bias:* whether negative signals are receiving disproportionate weight relative to positive ones of equivalent evidential strength, or vice versa.

**Contextual expansion**

*Recency bias:* whether the signal is being treated as more significant because it is new rather than because it is strong.

*Optimism or pessimism bias:* whether the founder's general disposition toward the business's prospects is colouring how the signal is being read.

For each bias assessed as present, identify what it is doing to the signal's interpreted weight and in which direction.

---

## Stage 6: Decision Implication Assessment

Establish what decision this signal would actually change if taken seriously.

This is the most important stage. A signal that does not change any decision is noise by definition, regardless of how interesting it is. A signal that would change a specific decision warrants the attention being given to it.

Ask:

- If this signal is real and represents a pattern, what would the founder do differently from what they are currently doing or planning?
- If this signal is noise or an outlier, what would stay the same?
- Is the decision it would change one the founder is currently facing, or is it a future decision that can be monitored over time?
- What is the cost of acting on this signal if it turns out to be noise?
- What is the cost of not acting on this signal if it turns out to be real?

The asymmetry between the cost of acting on noise and the cost of missing a real signal determines the appropriate response threshold. Where the cost of missing a real signal is high relative to the cost of acting on noise, the threshold for response should be lower. Where the costs are reversed, the threshold should be higher.

---

## Stage 7: The Recommendation

Produce a clear recommendation in one of three categories.

**Strategic response:** the signal is sufficiently corroborated, from a sufficiently representative source, and would change a sufficiently important decision, that it warrants an immediate strategic response. Specify what that response is.

**Monitoring brief:** the signal is plausible and potentially significant but not yet sufficiently corroborated to warrant a strategic response. Specify what additional information would be needed to upgrade it to a strategic response, and how the founder will actively monitor for that information over a defined timeframe.

**Active discounting:** the signal does not warrant strategic attention at this time. Specify the reasoning so the founder can revisit the assessment if the signal reappears in a different form or from a different source.

Active discounting is not dismissal. It is a documented decision not to act, with the reasoning recorded. This matters because signals that are discounted and then recur in a different form are often the most important ones.

---

## Stage 8: The Summary

Present this before the full staged analysis. It orients the founder to the shape of what they are about to engage with.

Use first principles thinking as the underlying architecture. Return to the candidate interpretations identified in Stage 2 and state which one the evidence most supports.

Structure the summary in three parts:

First, state what the signal most likely means, based on the evidence assessed, in one plain sentence.

Second, state the single most important factor in that assessment: the corroboration that confirmed it, the bias that was distorting it, or the source limitation that reduced its weight.

Third, state the recommendation category and the specific action or monitoring brief in one sentence.

The summary should be three sentences. The founder should understand the shape of the review before entering the detail.

---

## Stage 9: Framing for Others

Help the founder communicate the signal assessment to the relevant audience where appropriate. The output is drafted, not described. Apply Toulmin's argumentation structure throughout: every claim accompanied by its grounds, the warrant connecting them, and acknowledgment of the conditions under which the claim would not hold.

**Leadership team**
Where the signal has implications for team priorities or direction, the communication should share the signal, the assessment methodology, the recommendation, and the reasoning. Teams that understand how their leadership distinguishes signal from noise develop better judgment themselves over time.

**Board or investors**
Where the signal relates to market conditions, competitive dynamics, or strategic direction, the board communication should present the signal, the assessment, and what it does or does not change about the current plan. Boards are more useful to founders who surface and assess signals systematically than to founders who either ignore signals or overreact to them.

**Personal record**
Document the signal, the assessment, and the recommendation regardless of outcome. Signals that were correctly identified as noise are as valuable to record as signals that prompted strategic response, because the pattern of what turns out to be signal versus noise in this specific market and business is itself strategic information.

---

## Communication Principles

**Language calibration**
Translate findings into the specific language of this business and market. The assessment should feel like a rigorous examination of this signal, not a generic framework applied to it. Name the specific customer, competitor, or data point rather than referring to it abstractly.

**Observation separated from judgment**
The review assesses the signal, not the founder's judgment in noticing it or bringing it for review. There is no such thing as a signal not worth examining. The value of the skill is in the examination, not in whether the signal turns out to be significant.

**Progressive disclosure**
Lead with the recommendation. Make the full assessment available but do not bury the conclusion in the analysis. A founder reading the output should know the recommendation before they read the reasoning.

**The recommendation as anchor**
The founder leaves with a specific recommendation in one of three categories, with the reasoning made explicit. Not a balanced assessment of competing interpretations with no clear direction.
