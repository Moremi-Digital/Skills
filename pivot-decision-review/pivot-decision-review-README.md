# Pivot Decision Review

A structured assessment that determines whether the evidence supports a strategic pivot, what category of pivot it supports, and what the minimum viable test of the new direction is before full commitment.

The pivot question is almost always asked under the wrong conditions. Either reactively, when things are not working and pressure is building, or prematurely, when results are coming but more slowly than hoped. In both cases the decision quality is typically poor: it is made under emotional load, with a poorly defined problem, and without a reliable way to distinguish between a business that needs to change direction and a business that needs to execute better on its current one.

The most common and most costly error is pivoting away from a strategy that was correct but had not yet had time to compound. The second is persisting with a fundamentally wrong strategy because sunk cost and optimism make the evidence of failure easy to reinterpret as temporary. This skill is designed to resolve that ambiguity through structured assessment rather than instinct under pressure.

---

## Who it is for

Founders who are considering a pivot and want to examine the question rigorously before deciding. It is equally valuable for founders who are not sure whether they are facing a pivot question or an execution question, because the first move of the assessment is to resolve exactly that.

---

## The eight stages

**1. Situation Capture**
Collects the raw material: what is currently happening, how long it has been developing, what the original strategy was based on, what specifically is not working, what has already been tried, the founder's current hypothesis about the cause, and their instinct about the right pivot if they have one. The founder's hypothesis and instinct are made explicit at the outset so the review can engage with them directly.

**2. First Principles Decomposition**
Strips the situation back to the founding assumptions: what problem the business is trying to solve, for whom, and why this founder is the right person to solve it. Then asks whether what is not working is a symptom of those founding assumptions being wrong or a symptom of imperfect execution of a correct strategy. This distinction is the most important one in the assessment. A business with a correct strategy and imperfect execution needs better execution. A business with a fundamentally flawed strategy will not be saved by better execution.

**3. Execution vs Strategy Assessment**
The gate the rest of the assessment depends on. Assesses the evidence for whether the primary problem is execution or strategy, using a specific set of indicators for each. Where the evidence is ambiguous, assesses which interpretation is more likely to be a product of bias, since founders under pressure tend to attribute failure to execution rather than strategy because the strategy represents their original judgment.

**4. Pivot Category Assessment**
If the problem is primarily strategy, assesses which of four pivot categories the evidence most supports. The problem pivot: the market exists but the problem being solved is wrong. The solution pivot: the problem and customer are right but the current solution is not the right way to address the problem. The segment pivot: the problem and solution are viable but the target customer segment is wrong. The model pivot: the problem, solution, and segment are correct but the business model cannot generate viable unit economics. Each category has specific evidence indicators. Where more than one category is supported, assesses which is the primary constraint, because addressing the wrong one first is a common and costly error.

**5. Bias Assessment**
Assesses the cognitive biases most likely to be distorting both the decision to pivot and the assessment of which pivot to make. Runs a default scan of six biases: sunk cost influence, availability bias, overconfidence, optimism bias, narrative bias, and loss aversion. Expands contextually to social proof bias and recency bias. Each bias is assessed for whether it is pushing toward or away from the pivot and whether it is distorting the category assessment.

**6. Minimum Viable Test**
Designs the smallest, cheapest, fastest experiment that would provide meaningful evidence that the proposed pivot direction is correct, structured so the founder can run it without fully abandoning the current strategy before the new direction is validated. Each test specifies what it costs, what results would increase or decrease confidence, what the founder would do in each case, and how long the test needs to run. Pivots that cannot be tested before full commitment are named as irreducible bets and treated accordingly.

**7. The Summary**
Presented before the full staged analysis. Uses first principles thinking to return to the founding assumptions and state which the evidence says are still correct. Three to four sentences: whether the primary problem is execution or strategy with the most important supporting evidence, which pivot category the evidence supports if applicable, and the specific next action.

**8. Framing for Others**
Helps the founder communicate the pivot assessment to the relevant audience, drafted rather than described. Applies Toulmin's argumentation structure throughout. Three audience frames: co-founder or leadership team, board or investors, and personal record. The personal record is particularly important here: if the pivot does not work, it is the document that allows the founder to distinguish between a pivot correctly identified but poorly executed and one incorrectly identified from the outset.

## As a standalone framework

The execution versus strategy gate in Stage 3 is the most valuable standalone tool in this skill. Before any pivot conversation with a co-founder, board, or investor, answer this question first: is the evidence showing that the strategy is wrong, or that the execution of a correct strategy has been insufficient? The indicators in Stage 3 provide a structured way to assess that without the full review.

The four pivot categories in Stage 4 are a useful classification system for any strategic change conversation. Naming which category a proposed pivot falls into makes the discussion more precise and reduces the risk of addressing the wrong constraint.
