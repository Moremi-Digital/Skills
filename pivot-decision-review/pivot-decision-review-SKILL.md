# Pivot Decision Review

This skill exists to help founders determine whether the evidence supports a pivot and, if so, what kind. The pivot question is almost always asked under the wrong conditions: either reactively, when things are not working and pressure is building, or prematurely, when things are working but not as impressively as the founder hoped. In both cases, the quality of the decision is typically poor because it is made under emotional load, with a poorly defined problem, and without a way to distinguish between a business that needs to change direction and a business that needs to execute better on its current one.

The most common and most costly error is pivoting away from a strategy that was correct but had not yet had time to compound, because the signal that it was working had not yet become visible. The second most common error is persisting with a strategy that is fundamentally wrong because sunk cost and optimism make the evidence of failure easy to reinterpret as temporary.

This skill does not tell the founder whether to pivot. It tells them what the evidence actually says, what category of change the evidence supports if any, and what the minimum viable test of the proposed new direction is before full commitment.

Work through each stage in order. Every stage produces output before the next begins.

---

## Opening Orientation

Before beginning, establish the following in plain language:

This process examines whether the current situation is an execution problem, a strategy problem, or a fit problem, and what the evidence says about which it is. It does not evaluate the emotional state of the founder or the pressure of the external environment, except insofar as those are relevant to understanding what is distorting the assessment.

The output is a clear recommendation on whether to pivot, what kind of pivot the evidence supports if so, and what to test before committing. Not a balanced summary of considerations.

---

## Stage 1: Situation Capture

Collect the raw material before any analysis begins. Ask the founder to describe:

- What is currently happening in the business that is prompting the pivot question
- How long this situation has been developing
- What the original strategy was and what it was based on
- What specifically is not working: revenue, retention, acquisition, margin, team, or something else
- What has already been tried to address it and what happened
- What the founder's current hypothesis is about the cause
- What the founder's instinct about the right pivot is, if they have one

The founder's current hypothesis and instinct are made explicit at the outset so the review can assess them directly rather than having them operate as implicit conclusions throughout.

---

## Stage 2: First Principles Decomposition

Before assessing the pivot options, strip the situation back to the most fundamental questions.

Ask: what problem is this business trying to solve, for whom, and why is this founder the right person to solve it? These are the founding assumptions of the business. They may or may not still be correct.

Then ask: is what is currently not working a symptom of those founding assumptions being wrong, or is it a symptom of the execution of a correct strategy being imperfect?

This distinction is the most important one in the pivot assessment. A business with a correct strategy and imperfect execution needs better execution, not a new strategy. A business with a fundamentally flawed strategy will not be saved by better execution. The first principles decomposition is the mechanism for making that distinction before any pivot category assessment begins.

---

## Stage 3: Execution vs Strategy Assessment

Before examining pivot categories, determine whether the primary problem is execution or strategy. This is the gate that the rest of the assessment depends on.

Indicators that the problem is primarily execution:
- The strategy has worked in comparable businesses in comparable markets
- There are pockets of the business where the strategy is working, even if they are small
- The feedback from customers who have engaged deeply with the product is positive, but the challenge is reaching or converting more of them
- The team has not yet had time or resource to execute the strategy at the level it requires
- The metrics that matter most for validating the core hypothesis are moving in the right direction, even if slowly

Indicators that the problem is primarily strategy:
- The strategy has not worked despite sustained, competent execution
- There are no pockets where the strategy is working
- The feedback from customers who have engaged most deeply is negative on the core value proposition, not just on peripheral features
- The team has executed well but the results are not coming
- The metrics that would validate the core hypothesis are not moving despite reasonable effort

Where the evidence is genuinely ambiguous, assess which interpretation is more likely to be a product of bias: founders under pressure tend to attribute failure to execution rather than strategy because the strategy represents their original judgment. Assess whether that is operating here.

---

## Stage 4: Pivot Category Assessment

If Stage 3 concludes that the problem is primarily strategy, assess which of four pivot categories the evidence most supports.

**The problem pivot**
The market exists and is reachable, but the problem being solved is not the right one. Customers in the target segment have a genuine willingness to pay, but not for what is currently being built. They have a more pressing, more specific, or more clearly defined problem that the current product does not address.

Evidence that supports this category: customers engage with the product but do not convert or retain, customers tell you what they actually use the product for and it is different from what it was designed to do, the market is buying solutions in this space but not from this business.

**The solution pivot**
The problem is correctly identified and the target customer is right, but the current solution is not the most effective or efficient way to address the problem. The customer has the problem and would pay for a solution, but not this one.

Evidence that supports this category: customers articulate the problem in the terms the business uses, but reject the solution, customers buy partial solutions or workarounds rather than the full product, the core value proposition makes sense to customers conceptually but does not convert.

**The segment pivot**
The problem is correctly identified and the solution is viable, but the target customer segment is wrong. The product works for a different customer than the one currently being targeted.

Evidence that supports this category: there is a customer segment that uses and values the product at a disproportionately high rate relative to the target, the customers who do convert and retain are consistently different from the target customer in some meaningful way, the target customer consistently deprioritises or devalues what the product offers while a different segment would not.

**The model pivot**
The problem, solution, and segment are all correctly identified, but the business model cannot generate the unit economics required for the business to be viable. The product works, the customer values it, but the current approach to pricing, channel, cost structure, or revenue model makes the business unsustainable at the margins it can achieve.

Evidence that supports this category: customers value the product and convert, but willingness to pay does not support the cost structure, the current channel or acquisition approach does not produce viable unit economics even at scale, a different pricing or delivery model would produce materially better economics for the same value proposition.

Identify which category the evidence most supports. If the evidence supports more than one category, assess which is the primary constraint, because addressing the wrong one first is a common and costly error.

---

## Stage 5: Bias Assessment

Assess the cognitive biases most likely to be distorting both the decision to pivot and the assessment of which pivot to make.

**Default scan**

*Sunk cost influence:* whether prior investment in the current strategy, financial, reputational, or emotional, is making the founder more reluctant to acknowledge that a pivot is warranted, or conversely, making them more eager to pivot to something new to escape the feeling of loss.

*Availability bias:* whether a recent failure, a competitor's success with a different model, or a vivid conversation with an investor is driving the pivot consideration more than the systematic evidence of what is and is not working.

*Overconfidence:* whether the founder's confidence in the proposed pivot direction is proportionate to the evidence for it, or whether it reflects the same level of certainty that accompanied the original strategy.

*Optimism bias:* whether the assessment of what the pivot will achieve is more favourable than comparable pivots in comparable situations would suggest.

*Narrative bias:* whether the founder has already constructed a compelling story about why the pivot makes sense, and whether that story is shaping the assessment of the evidence rather than emerging from it.

*Loss aversion:* whether the pivot is being driven more by wanting to avoid further loss than by genuine evidence that the new direction is superior to the current one with better execution.

**Contextual expansion**

*Social proof bias:* whether the pivot is partly driven by what other founders, investors, or competitors are doing rather than by what the evidence says is right for this business.

*Recency bias:* whether a recent bad period is being weighted more heavily than the full history of the business's evidence in either direction.

For each bias assessed as present, identify whether it is pushing toward or away from the pivot and whether it is distorting the pivot category assessment.

---

## Stage 6: Minimum Viable Test

If the assessment concludes that a pivot is warranted, design the minimum viable test of the proposed new direction before full commitment.

The test should be the smallest, cheapest, fastest experiment that would provide meaningful evidence that the pivot direction is correct. It should be possible to run it without fully abandoning the current strategy, so that the founder is not in the position of having committed to a new direction before it has been validated.

For each proposed test, specify:

- What the test is
- What it costs in time and resource
- What result would increase confidence that the pivot is correct
- What result would decrease confidence
- What the founder would do in each case
- How long the test needs to run before the results are meaningful

A pivot that cannot be tested before full commitment is an irreducible bet. Name it as such and apply the same treatment as in the Strategic Assumption Review: conscious acceptance with explicit acknowledgment of what the downside looks like if the pivot direction proves incorrect.

---

## Stage 7: The Summary

Present this before the full staged analysis.

Use first principles thinking as the underlying architecture. Return to the founding assumptions identified in Stage 2 and state clearly which of them the evidence says are still correct and which are not.

Structure the summary in three parts:

First, state clearly whether the primary problem is execution or strategy, in one plain sentence, with the single most important piece of evidence that supports that conclusion.

Second, if the problem is strategy, state which pivot category the evidence most supports and why.

Third, state the recommended next action: the specific test to run before committing, or, if the evidence is already sufficient for commitment, the specific first step of the pivot with the success indicator that will confirm it is working.

The summary should be three to four sentences. The founder should understand the shape of the assessment before entering the detail.

---

## Stage 8: Framing for Others

Help the founder communicate the pivot assessment to the relevant audience. The output is drafted, not described. Apply Toulmin's argumentation structure throughout: every claim accompanied by its grounds, the warrant connecting them, and acknowledgment of the conditions under which the claim would not hold.

**Co-founder or leadership team**
The emphasis is on what the evidence says, what the assessment concluded, what the proposed test is, and what the decision criteria are for proceeding with the full pivot after testing. The team needs to understand the reasoning, not just the direction, because a pivot that the team understands will be executed more effectively than one they are following on faith.

**Board or investors**
The emphasis is on the rigour of the assessment, what the evidence shows about the current situation, what the proposed pivot addresses and why, and what the test plan is before full commitment. Boards and investors are more useful to founders who can demonstrate structured thinking about a difficult strategic question than to founders who announce a pivot without showing how they arrived at the conclusion.

**Personal record**
Document the full assessment, the evidence that supported the conclusion, the pivot category identified, the test design, and the decision criteria. If the pivot does not work, this record allows the founder to distinguish between a pivot that was correctly identified but poorly executed and one that was incorrectly identified from the outset.

---

## Communication Principles

**Clarity over comfort**
The pivot assessment produces a clear recommendation, not a balanced summary of considerations. The founder already has access to uncertainty. What they need is the clearest possible read of what the evidence says. If the evidence says it is an execution problem, say that directly. If it says the strategy is wrong, say that directly.

**Observation separated from judgment**
The assessment examines the evidence, not the quality of the original strategic judgment. A strategy that turns out to be wrong was not necessarily a bad decision when it was made. The assessment is forward-facing.

**Progressive disclosure**
Lead with the execution versus strategy conclusion. Everything else follows from that. A founder who has to read through the full pivot category assessment before knowing whether there is a strategic problem at all will interpret the detail through the lens of anxiety rather than clarity.

**The test as anchor**
The founder leaves with a specific, designed test of the proposed pivot direction, or with a conscious acceptance of an irreducible bet if testing is not possible. Not a pivot decision made without validation.
