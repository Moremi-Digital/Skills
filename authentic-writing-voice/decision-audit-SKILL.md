name: decision-audit
description: >
  Apply this skill when a founder or senior leader wants to audit a past decision. Triggers include: "help me review a decision I made," "I want to understand what went wrong," "audit this decision," "help me think through what happened," "I need to explain this decision to my board/team," or any request to retrospectively examine a consequential choice. This skill works through a structured sequence from decision capture through to framing for others. It separates decision quality from outcome quality, surfaces assumptions, maps information behaviour, assesses reasoning and bias, evaluates risk, identifies patterns across multiple decisions, and produces a reconstruction and communication output. It is domain-agnostic and applies equally to strategic, operational, financial, hiring, and product decisions.
---

# Decision Audit Skill

This skill exists to help founders and senior leaders examine the quality of their reasoning on past decisions, independent of how those decisions turned out. It operates as a structured sequence. Work through each stage in order. Do not skip stages or conflate them.

Every stage produces output before moving to the next. The leader's responses at each stage inform the analysis at subsequent stages.

---

## Opening Orientation

Before beginning the audit, establish the following with the leader in plain language:

This process examines the quality of the reasoning behind the decision, not the decision itself and not the person who made it. A good decision can produce a bad outcome. A bad decision can get lucky. The audit separates these two things so that learning is grounded in reasoning quality rather than result.

The output is not a verdict. It is a map of how the decision was made and what a more rigorous version of that process would have looked like, given what was actually known at the time.

---

## Stage 1: Decision Capture

Collect the raw material before any analysis begins. Ask the leader to provide:

- What the decision was, stated as precisely as possible
- When it was made and who made it
- What was known at the time of the decision
- What was unknown at the time but could have been known with more effort
- What the decision was stated as trying to achieve
- What the decision structure, in hindsight, reveals it was actually optimising for (this is often different from the stated objective and should be surfaced explicitly, not assumed to match)
- What happened as a result

The gap between stated objective and actual optimisation is frequently where the most important finding lives. Do not accept the stated framing at face value. If the decision structure reveals a different underlying optimisation, name it clearly and ask the leader to confirm or correct.

---

## Stage 2: Assumption Mapping

Extract the assumptions embedded in the decision and classify each one.

For each assumption identified, establish:

- Whether it was tested before the decision was made, or taken on faith
- Whether it was within the leader's control to test it at the time
- Whether it was load-bearing: the decision fails or changes materially if this assumption is wrong
- Whether it was peripheral: the decision holds even if this assumption proves incorrect

Assumptions that are both load-bearing and untested represent the highest-risk category. Identify these explicitly.

Do not conflate assumptions with facts. An assumption is something that was treated as sufficiently certain to act on, regardless of whether it was verified.

---

## Stage 3: Information Audit

Examine the information behaviour at the time of the decision. This is distinct from assumption mapping. Assumptions concern what was believed. Information audit concerns what was known, sought, available, and used.

Assess:

- What information was actively used in reaching the decision
- What information was available at the time but was ignored, deprioritised, or not consulted
- What information was sought but could not be obtained
- What information was not sought but should have been, given what was knowable at the time

The standard is not hindsight. The question is whether the information behaviour was appropriate given what was reasonably knowable at the time of the decision, not what turned out to be true afterward.

---

## Stage 4: Reasoning Quality and Bias Assessment

This is the core of the audit. It examines the logical structure connecting available information to the decision made, and identifies cognitive biases that may have distorted that structure.

**Reasoning quality**

Assess:

- Whether the conclusion reached was proportionate to the evidence available
- Whether alternatives to the chosen course were genuinely considered or whether the decision was effectively made before deliberation began
- Whether dissenting views were present in the process and, if so, how they were handled
- Whether the decision was reversible or irreversible, and whether it was treated with the appropriate level of deliberation for its category. Irreversible decisions warrant a materially higher standard of deliberation than reversible ones. Conflating the two is a common and costly error.

**Bias assessment: default scan**

Run the following six biases as a baseline assessment for every audit, regardless of the decision type:

*Confirmation bias:* whether information supporting the preferred option received disproportionate weight relative to information that challenged it.

*Sunk cost influence:* whether prior investment in a direction, financial, reputational, or temporal, made reversal feel costlier than a rational assessment of future value would warrant.

*Availability bias:* whether recent, vivid, or emotionally salient experiences distorted the probability assessment of outcomes relative to the actual base rate.

*Overconfidence:* whether the stated or implied confidence level in the decision was proportionate to the actual evidence base, or exceeded it.

*Authority bias:* whether the decision deferred to seniority or perceived expertise in a way that bypassed independent assessment of the underlying reasoning.

*In-group bias:* whether the people consulted in reaching the decision were too similar in perspective, function, or interest to provide genuine challenge.

**Bias assessment: contextual expansion**

Beyond the default six, assess whether the specific details of this decision activate additional biases. Apply the following where the decision context warrants it:

*Planning fallacy:* where the decision involved timelines, budgets, or resource estimates that proved systematically optimistic.

*Bandwagon effect:* where the direction was influenced by what competitors, peers, or the market were doing, without independent assessment of whether that direction was appropriate for this specific situation.

*Anchoring:* where an initial reference point, a price, a valuation, a competitor benchmark, a prior figure, materially influenced the final decision in a way that may not have been warranted.

*Framing effect:* where the way a choice was presented, as a gain or a loss, as a percentage or an absolute number, as a risk or an opportunity, influenced the direction taken independent of the underlying substance.

*Optimism bias:* distinct from overconfidence, this concerns the systematic over-weighting of positive scenarios relative to what the base rate of comparable situations would suggest.

*Status quo bias:* where inaction or continuation of the existing path was the effective default, and the evidential bar for change was set higher than the circumstances required.

This list is not exhaustive. If the conversation surfaces evidence of a bias not listed here, assess it with the same rigour. Only surface biases that are genuinely evidenced in the decision detail. Do not apply the full taxonomy as a checklist regardless of relevance.

For each bias assessed as present, establish what effect it had on the direction of the decision. Naming a bias without assessing its effect is not useful.

---

## Stage 5: Risk Assessment

Risk is assessed in two parts.

**At the time of the decision**

Establish:

- What downside scenarios were identified before the decision was made
- How each was weighted relative to the upside
- What mitigation was built into the decision or consciously foregone, and why

**At the outcome stage**

Classify the risks that materialised or that are now visible into three categories:

*Risks that were identified and accepted:* the leader knew this could happen, weighed it, and proceeded. This is not a failure. It is a decision under uncertainty made with appropriate awareness.

*Risks that were identified but underweighted:* the leader was aware of the downside but assigned it lower probability or lower impact than it warranted. This is a calibration problem and points to specific adjustments in how similar risks are weighted in future.

*Risks that were not identified at all:* the leader had no awareness of this downside scenario at the time of the decision. This is a structural gap in the decision process and warrants examination of why the scenario was not surfaced, whether through information behaviour, assumption, bias, or process design.

Each category carries different implications for what needs to change. Keep them clearly separated.

---

## Stage 6: Outcome Separation

Explicitly separate the quality of the decision from the quality of the outcome. Assign each an independent assessment.

A well-reasoned decision that produced a poor outcome due to factors outside the leader's control should be identified as such. The reasoning process was sound even if the result was not.

A poorly-reasoned decision that produced a good outcome should be identified as such. The result does not validate the process.

If leaders update their decision-making on outcomes rather than reasoning quality, they improve when they get lucky and degrade when they do not. The audit exists to break that pattern.

State the outcome assessment plainly and without mitigation.

---

## Stage 7: Pattern Identification

This stage is activated once the leader has audited more than one decision using this skill.

Across the decisions audited, identify:

- The conditions under which this leader's reasoning tends to be strongest: decision types, information environments, team compositions, time horizons
- The conditions under which reasoning tends to be weakest or most susceptible to bias: time pressure, high emotional stakes, decisions involving particular people or functions, situations requiring the leader to override a prior public commitment, decisions at the boundary of their direct expertise

Patterns are more valuable than individual audit findings because they are actionable at the level of process design. A leader who knows they consistently underweight downside risk under time pressure can build a structural check into high-velocity decisions. A leader who knows their information behaviour degrades when a trusted advisor is absent can build a compensating process. Name the pattern specifically enough to be actionable.

---

## Stage 8: The Reconstruction

Produce a higher-quality version of the decision process as it could have been run, given what was actually known at the time of the decision.

This is not a judgment about what the right answer was. It is a map of what more rigorous deliberation would have looked like: which assumptions would have been tested, which information would have been sought, which biases would have been checked, which risks would have been weighted differently, and how the deliberation structure would have been designed given the reversibility of the decision.

The reconstruction is the output the leader carries forward into future decisions in the same category. It should be specific enough to be usable, not a generic call for more rigour.

Close the analytical portion of the audit here. The reconstruction is the anchor. Do not follow it with a summary of errors.

---

## Stage 9: The Summary

Present this before delivering the full staged analysis, not after. It orients the leader to the shape of what they are about to engage with.

The summary uses first principles thinking as its underlying architecture without naming it as such. Strip the decision back to its most fundamental components before any evaluative framing, so that the analysis operates on the root of the decision rather than the narrative the leader has constructed around it.

Structure the summary in three parts:

First, restate the fundamental problem the decision was trying to solve, in plain language, at its root. This strips away the specifics that can obscure what the decision was actually a response to.

Second, state whether the decision addressed that root problem directly, or whether it addressed a symptom, a proxy, or a more comfortable version of it. This is often the most important finding and it emerges naturally from the first principles restatement.

Third, state the single most important finding from the audit in one sentence. Not a list. One finding: the one with the highest implications for how this leader makes similar decisions in future.

The summary should be two to three sentences in total. The leader should be able to read it and understand the shape of the audit before entering the detail.

Apply progressive disclosure throughout the full audit output. Foreground the two or three findings with the highest implications. Make the fuller analysis available but do not lead with volume.

---

## Stage 10: Framing for Others

Help the leader construct a clear, honest account of the decision for a specific audience. This is built on the full audit findings, not on the leader's narrative memory of the decision. It supports transparent communication, not justification or mitigation.

Establish which audience the leader needs to address and apply the corresponding frame:

**Board or investors**
Emphasis on process integrity, what the audit revealed about the quality of deliberation, what was learned, and what changes to decision governance or process architecture follow as a result. The board needs confidence in how decisions of this type will be made going forward, not a defence of the outcome.

**Senior team**
Emphasis on what the decision was trying to achieve, what it revealed about how the organisation makes calls under pressure or uncertainty, and what the practical implications are for how similar decisions get structured going forward. The team needs to understand what changes and why, not to receive a post-hoc rationalisation.

**External stakeholders such as clients or partners**
Emphasis on outcome, accountability, and forward direction. Internal process detail is not relevant to this audience unless it directly affects them. Clarity and forward orientation take precedence over completeness.

**Personal record**
A full written account of the audit findings in a form the leader can retrieve when facing a similar decision category in future. This is not a communication to others. It is a thinking asset for the leader's own use, capturing the reconstruction, the pattern findings, and the bias and risk assessments in retrievable form.

The framing output should be drafted, not described. Produce the actual communication, not instructions for how to write it.

The boundary between explanation and justification is explicit throughout. If the audit identified a meaningful reasoning failure, the framing output helps the leader acknowledge it clearly. Leaders who communicate their own decision failures with transparency build more durable trust than those who explain them away.

---

## Communication Principles

These apply throughout every stage of the audit.

**Language calibration**
Calibrate output to the leader's own language and frame of reference. Translate findings into the specific context of the decision being audited. "Confirmation bias was present" is less useful than "the information gathered before this decision came almost entirely from sources already aligned with the direction you were leaning, and the one dissenting view in the room was noted but not explored." Specificity in context is what makes a finding land.

**Observation separated from judgment**
The skill audits reasoning, not the person. Frame output as a thinking partner reconstructing the decision process alongside the leader, not as an evaluator passing verdict. This distinction must be maintained throughout, not just stated at the opening.

**Progressive disclosure**
Surface the two or three findings with the highest implications for future decisions in the same category. Present these clearly. Make the fuller analysis available on request. Do not lead with volume. A decision audit that surfaces six biases, three information gaps, and two assumption failures simultaneously is accurate but unusable.

**The reconstruction as anchor**
The reconstruction closes the analytical work. The leader leaves with what could have been done differently and why, not with a catalogue of errors. The register is generative throughout.

**Plain-English summary first**
The summary precedes the full analysis. The leader should understand the shape of the audit before entering the detail.
