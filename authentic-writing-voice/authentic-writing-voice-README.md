# Authentic Writing Voice

A prohibitions-based skill that removes AI writing patterns from any content before it is published.

The problem this skill addresses is specific: AI-assisted writing has produced a recognisable set of structural and stylistic patterns that signal templated thinking to any reader who has spent time on LinkedIn or reading business content. These patterns are not wrong because they are AI-generated. They are wrong because they substitute structural confidence for actual argument. Short sentences, punchy rhythm, pseudo-revelation hooks, and balanced closers can all make weak ideas sound assured. Strip the formatting away and the idea often does not hold.

This skill enforces a complete set of prohibitions against those patterns. It applies them without exception, to new content and to existing drafts.

---

## Who it is for

Anyone producing written content for publication or external reading: LinkedIn posts, articles, blog posts, social media captions, email copy, marketing content, thought leadership pieces, proposals, or any prose where voice and authenticity matter.

It is particularly useful for founders and senior leaders who use AI to assist with content but do not want their output to read as AI-generated, and for anyone whose credibility depends on their thinking being legible and distinct.

---

## The prohibitions

### Structural prohibitions

**No em dashes**
Em dashes are statistically over-represented in AI output and are a primary tell. Restructure the sentence, or use a colon, semicolon, or comma as appropriate.

**No staccato sentences as rhetorical punch**
No sequences of deliberately short, stripped-back sentences stacked for percussive impact. Prose builds argument with controlled, flowing construction. Sentences should not be fragmented artificially to manufacture drama or momentum.

Examples of what this prohibits:
- "Not just confused. Actually scared."
- "It doesn't."
- "They post. They outreach. They follow up."

**No pseudo-revelation hooks**
Phrases like "Here's the uncomfortable truth" or "Here's why it matters" perform the promise of insight without earning it. If there is an insight, state it. Do not announce that one is coming.

**No fabricated anecdotal openers**
"So I was doing X the other day" simulates relatability through manufactured narrative. If an anecdote is real and relevant, it can be used, but it must be grounded in specific, verifiable detail, not constructed to warm the reader up.

**No pseudo-contemplative ellipsis**
Trailing dots that perform thoughtfulness rather than reflect it. If a thought is unfinished, finish it or cut it.

**No faux-candor pivots**
"And honestly?" or "I'm going to be straight with you" signal intimacy formulaically. They imply that what preceded was less than honest, and they substitute a register of candor for the thing itself.

**No motivational or hustle-culture register**
No pseudo-profound content that sounds substantial but carries no specific claim. Language that functions as inspiration without argument: "the work is the work," "show up every day," "trust the process." If there is a point, make it precisely.

**No templated antithesis**
"It's not X, it's Y" is a structural tic, not an argument. If a distinction is worth drawing, explain why X is wrong and why Y is more accurate. Do not rely on the pattern to do the work.

**No hyperbolic declaratives**
"This changes everything" and equivalents. If something is significant, demonstrate its significance through the argument. Do not assert it.

**No phatic filler**
Phrases that signal an emotional register without advancing the actual meaning of the sentence: "And that's the thing," "Here's what I know," "The truth is." If the sentence that follows is true, begin with that sentence.

---

### Sentence-level prohibitions

Never begin any piece of writing with the word "Most." This includes "Most people," "Most founders," "Most businesses," "Most marketers," and any similar universal-group opening.

Do not open with a universal claim about what a group of people does wrong. Positioning the writer as elevated above a failing herd has been so thoroughly automated that it no longer reads as insight.

Do not use the "Not X. Not Y. Not Z." negative list structure to build suspense before a reveal. This is a structural template, not a rhetorical choice.

Do not use the "Not because X. Because Y." pivot as a standalone construction. It mimics depth by appearing to self-correct, but it is a template.

Do not end writing with two balanced opposing statements designed to feel conclusive. If there is a genuine contrast to draw, it should emerge from the argument, not be dropped in as a closing flourish.

Do not use "quietly" or "in silence" as a metaphor for hidden risk or slow failure: "collapses quietly," "losing money quietly." This construction functions as an AI watermark.

Do not insert a compressed career arc mid-content to establish authority. Credibility is demonstrated through the quality of the thinking, not asserted through a shorthand résumé drop.

Do not instruct the reader to save, bookmark, or return to the content. If the content has value, let it stand.

Do not close with a rhetorical question that reflects the post's central metaphor back at the reader as a self-diagnostic engagement mechanic.

---

## The underlying test

These patterns share a common failure: structural confidence masking conceptual thinness.

Strip the formatting away and ask whether the argument still holds. If the idea only works because of the rhythm, rewrite it until the idea carries its own weight.

Write to think, not to perform thinking.

## How to use it with Claude

1. Open [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai)
2. Start a new conversation
3. Upload `SKILL.md` from this folder using the attachment icon
4. Ask Claude to write, draft, or review content. The skill applies automatically.

**For generating new content:** Claude drafts the piece, scans it against every prohibition before finalising, and restructures any section where a pattern appears. It does not patch with synonyms; it rewrites the logic so the pattern cannot re-emerge.

**For reviewing existing content:** Claude flags each prohibition violated, identifies the specific passage and the rule it breaks, and offers a rewritten version that carries the same idea without the prohibited pattern.

---

## As a standalone checklist

You do not need Claude to use this framework. The prohibitions above serve as a review checklist for any piece of writing before it is published. Read the draft, run it against each prohibition, and rewrite any passage that triggers one.

The underlying test applies regardless of tool: strip the formatting and ask whether the argument holds.
