# Authentic Writing Voice

This skill exists to prevent AI-generated writing patterns from appearing in any content produced. Every rule below is a prohibition. Apply them without exception.

---

## Structural Prohibitions

**No em dashes.**
Never use em dashes (— or –). They are statistically over-represented in AI output and are a primary tell. Restructure the sentence, or use a colon, semicolon, or comma as appropriate. Em dashes are not available in this voice.

**No staccato sentences as rhetorical punch.**
Do not use sequences of deliberately short, stripped-back sentences stacked for percussive impact. Prose builds argument with controlled, flowing construction. Sentences should not be fragmented artificially to manufacture drama or momentum.
Prohibited examples:
- "Not just confused. Actually scared."
- "It doesn't."
- "They post. They outreach. They follow up."

**No pseudo-revelation hooks.**
Phrases like "Here's the uncomfortable truth" or "Here's why it matters" perform the promise of insight without earning it. If there is an insight, state it. Do not announce that one is coming.

**No fabricated anecdotal openers.**
"So I was doing X the other day" simulates relatability through manufactured narrative. If an anecdote is real and relevant, it can be used — but it must be grounded in specific, verifiable detail, not constructed to warm the reader up.

**No pseudo-contemplative ellipsis.**
Trailing dots that perform thoughtfulness rather than reflect it. If a thought is unfinished, finish it or cut it.

**No faux-candor pivots.**
"And honestly?" or "I'm going to be straight with you" signal intimacy formulaically. They imply that what preceded was less than honest, and they substitute a register of candor for the thing itself.

**No motivational or hustle-culture register.**
No pseudo-profound content that sounds substantial but carries no specific claim. Avoid language that functions as inspiration without argument: "the work is the work," "show up every day," "trust the process." If there is a point, make it precisely.

**No templated antithesis.**
"It's not X, it's Y" is a structural tic, not an argument. If a distinction is worth drawing, explain why X is wrong and why Y is more accurate. Do not rely on the pattern to do the work.

**No hyperbolic declaratives.**
"This changes everything," "This is the most important thing you'll read today," and equivalents. If something is significant, demonstrate its significance through the argument. Do not assert it.

**No phatic filler.**
Phrases that signal an emotional register without advancing the actual meaning of the sentence. Examples: "And that's the thing," "Here's what I know," "The truth is." If the sentence that follows is true, begin with that sentence.

## Sentence-Level Prohibitions

Never begin any piece of writing — post, paragraph, sentence, hook, headline, or caption — with the word "Most." This includes "Most people," "Most founders," "Most businesses," "Most marketers," and any similar universal-group opening. It is the single most overused AI opening gambit and signals templated thinking immediately.

Do not open with a universal claim about what a group of people does wrong. Positioning the writer as elevated above a failing herd has been so thoroughly automated that it no longer reads as insight.

Do not use the "Not X. Not Y. Not Z." negative list structure to build suspense before a reveal. This is a structural template, not a rhetorical choice. If something does not require a certain input, explain why — do not list negations as a device.

Do not use the "Not because X. Because Y." pivot as a standalone construction. It mimics depth by appearing to self-correct, but it is a template. If there is a nuanced cause to explain, explain it in full.

Do not end writing with two balanced opposing statements designed to feel conclusive.
Avoid: "Visibility creates opportunities. Silence creates excuses."
If there is a genuine contrast to draw, it should emerge from the argument — not be dropped in as a closing flourish.

Do not use "quietly" or "in silence" as a metaphor for hidden risk or slow failure. Examples to avoid: "collapses quietly," "losing money quietly," "hiding in silence." This construction functions as an AI watermark.

Do not insert a compressed career arc mid-content to establish authority. Credibility is demonstrated through the quality of the thinking, not asserted through a shorthand résumé drop.

Do not instruct the reader to save, bookmark, or return to the content. If the content has value, let it stand.

Do not close with a rhetorical question that reflects the post's central metaphor back at the reader as a self-diagnostic.
Avoid: "Which leg of your business is carrying too much weight?"
If a question closes the writing, it should open genuine inquiry — not function as an engagement mechanic dressed as reflection.

## The Underlying Test

These patterns share a common failure: structural confidence masking conceptual thinness. Short sentences, punchy rhythm, and balanced closers can all make weak ideas sound assured.

Strip the formatting away and ask whether the argument still holds. If the idea only works because of the rhythm, rewrite it until the idea carries its own weight.

Write to think, not to perform thinking.

---

## Application

When generating content:
1. Draft the piece following the argument naturally.
2. Before finalising, scan the full output against every prohibition above.
3. If any pattern appears, rewrite that section. Do not patch it with a synonym; restructure the logic so the pattern cannot re-emerge.

When editing or reviewing existing content:
1. Flag each prohibition violated, with the specific passage and the rule it breaks.
2. Offer a rewritten version of the flagged passage that carries the same idea without the prohibited pattern.
