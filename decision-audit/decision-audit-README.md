# Decision Audit

A structured audit framework for examining the quality of reasoning behind a past decision, independent of how it turned out.

Most post-mortems focus on outcomes. This skill focuses on the reasoning process that produced the decision, before the outcome is allowed to colour the analysis. A good decision can produce a bad outcome. A bad decision can get lucky. Without separating the two, you update on results rather than reasoning, which degrades decision-making over time rather than improving it.

---

## What this skill does

The Decision Audit works through ten stages in sequence, from capturing the raw details of the decision through to helping you frame it for others. Each stage produces output before the next begins, and the findings at each stage inform the analysis that follows.

The output is not a verdict on the decision or the person who made it. It is a map of how the decision was made and what a more rigorous version of that process would have looked like, given what was actually known at the time.

---

## Who it is for

Founders and senior leaders making consequential decisions across any domain: strategic, operational, financial, hiring, product, or organisational. The skill is domain-agnostic. The quality of reasoning follows the same principles regardless of the subject matter.

---

## The ten stages

**1. Decision Capture**
Collects the raw material before any analysis begins: what the decision was, when it was made, what was known and unknown at the time, what it was stated as trying to achieve, what it was actually optimising for, and what happened. The gap between stated objective and actual optimisation is frequently where the most important finding lives.

**2. Assumption Mapping**
Extracts and classifies every assumption embedded in the decision. Each assumption is assessed for whether it was tested or taken on faith, whether it was within the leader's control to test, and whether it was load-bearing (the decision fails if this assumption is wrong) or peripheral. Load-bearing untested assumptions are the highest-risk category.

**3. Information Audit**
Examines what information was used, what was available but not consulted, what was sought but unavailable, and what was not sought but should have been. The standard is not hindsight. The question is whether the information behaviour was appropriate given what was reasonably knowable at the time.

**4. Reasoning Quality and Bias Assessment**
The core of the audit. Examines whether the conclusion was proportionate to the evidence, whether alternatives were genuinely considered, how dissenting views were handled, and whether the decision's reversibility was treated with the appropriate standard of deliberation.

Runs a default scan of six biases on every audit: confirmation bias, sunk cost influence, availability bias, overconfidence, authority bias, and in-group bias. Expands to additional biases where the specific decision context warrants it, including planning fallacy, bandwagon effect, anchoring, framing effect, optimism bias, and status quo bias. Only biases genuinely evidenced in the decision detail are surfaced, and each is assessed for its effect on the direction of the decision, not merely named.

**5. Risk Assessment**
Examines what downside scenarios were identified before the decision was made, how they were weighted, and what mitigation was built in or foregone. At the outcome stage, classifies risks into three categories with distinct implications: risks identified and accepted, risks identified but underweighted, and risks not identified at all.

**6. Outcome Separation**
Explicitly separates the quality of the decision from the quality of the outcome, assigning each an independent assessment. A well-reasoned decision that produced a poor outcome is identified as such. A poorly-reasoned decision that produced a good outcome is identified as such.

**7. Pattern Identification**
Activated once more than one decision has been audited. Identifies the conditions under which this leader's reasoning tends to be strongest and the conditions under which it tends to fail. Patterns are named specifically enough to be actionable at the level of process design, not just retrospective reflection.

**8. The Reconstruction**
Produces a higher-quality version of the decision process as it could have been run, based on what was actually known at the time. This is the output the leader carries forward into future decisions in the same category. It closes the analytical work. The audit does not end on a catalogue of errors.

**9. The Summary**
Presented before the full staged analysis, not after. Uses first principles thinking as its underlying architecture to strip the decision back to its root before any evaluative framing. Three parts: the fundamental problem the decision was trying to solve, whether the decision addressed that root problem or a proxy of it, and the single most important finding from the audit in one sentence.

**10. Framing for Others**
Helps the leader construct a clear, honest account of the decision for a specific audience, built on the full audit findings rather than narrative memory. Four audience frames: board or investors, senior team, external stakeholders, and personal record. The output is drafted, not described. The skill supports transparent communication, not justification or mitigation.

## How to use it

1. Open [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai)
2. Start a new conversation
3. Upload `SKILL.md` from this folder using the attachment icon
4. Tell Claude you want to audit a decision. Claude will work through the stages with you, asking for what it needs at each step.

You do not need to prepare anything in advance. The skill guides the conversation from the first prompt.

---

## As a standalone reference

If you want to use the Decision Audit framework without Claude, the ten stages above serve as a checklist for structured self-reflection or a facilitated session with a team or advisor. The bias list in Stage 4 is a useful standalone reference for any high-stakes deliberation, applied before a decision is made rather than after.
