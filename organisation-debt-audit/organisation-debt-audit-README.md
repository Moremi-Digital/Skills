# Organisation Debt Audit

A structured audit that maps the structural and operational liabilities a growing organisation has accumulated, prioritises them by growth impact, and produces specific recommendations for resolution.

Technical debt is a well-understood concept in software development. Organisational debt operates on the same principle and compounds in the same way. Every structural decision made in the early stages of a business, a reporting line added for convenience, a process built around one person's working style, a team that formed around a product that no longer exists, a title given to retain someone that now misrepresents their actual role, creates a liability that grows as the company scales. By the time founders notice it, it is expensive to unwind.

The goal of this audit is not a restructuring plan. It is a prioritised map of the debt the organisation is currently carrying, with a clear assessment of which items are actively limiting growth and which can be deferred, so the founder can make informed decisions about where to invest repair effort.

---

## Who it is for

Founders and senior leaders of growing organisations, particularly at inflection points: after a significant funding round, during a scaling phase, after a strategic pivot, or when execution friction has become noticeable but its source is not yet clear. The audit is most valuable run annually, before structural problems compound to the point where addressing them requires significant disruption.

---

## The eight stages

**1. Organisation Capture**
Collects the raw material: the current structure, when it was last deliberately designed, what has changed in the business since then, where the formal structure and the actual working structure diverge, and where friction is highest. The gap between the org chart and how work actually gets done is where the most significant debt is typically found.

**2. First Principles Decomposition**
Establishes what the organisation's structure should be optimising for at this specific stage of the business, in concrete terms rather than in general principles. Then asks whether the current structure optimises for that or for something the business needed at an earlier stage. This reorients the entire audit around what the structure needs to do now rather than what it was built to do.

**3. Debt Mapping**
Maps organisational debt across five categories: structural debt (reporting lines and team boundaries that reflect the past), role debt (titles and definitions that no longer match what people do), process debt (processes that have become ritual rather than function), decision debt (decision-making patterns that are too slow, too centralised, or too unclear), and cultural debt (norms that were functional at an earlier stage but are now limiting). For each item identified, notes which category it falls into, when it was approximately created, what it was serving at the time, and what it is currently costing.

**4. Bias Assessment**
Assesses the cognitive biases most likely to be sustaining the debt rather than prompting its resolution. Runs a default scan of six biases: sunk cost influence, status quo bias, authority bias, availability bias, overconfidence, and in-group bias. Expands contextually to include endowment effect and optimism bias where the specific situation warrants it. Each bias assessed as present is mapped to the specific items of debt it is most likely sustaining.

**5. Prioritisation**
Prioritises every item of debt across two dimensions: growth impact (how much it is limiting the organisation's ability to execute) and remediation effort (how much time, resource, and disruption addressing it would require). The highest-priority items are those with high growth impact and low or medium remediation effort. Items with high growth impact and high remediation effort go on the strategic roadmap. Items with low growth impact are deprioritised regardless of effort.

**6. The Reconstruction**
For each high-priority item, produces a specific recommendation: what the current state is, what it is costing, what the improved state would look like, and what the path from current to improved state requires. Recommendations are specific enough to act on. "Clarify decision rights" is not a recommendation. A named change to a named process with named owners and a named success indicator is.

**7. The Summary**
Presented before the full staged analysis. Uses first principles thinking to return to what the structure should be optimising for and state clearly whether the current structure is enabling or impeding that. Three sentences: what the structure needs to do at this stage, the single most significant item of debt identified, and the recommended immediate action.

**8. Framing for Others**
Helps the founder communicate the audit findings to the relevant audience, drafted rather than described. Applies Toulmin's argumentation structure throughout. Three audience frames: leadership team, board or investors, and personal record.

## As a standalone framework

The five debt categories in Stage 3 serve as a review checklist for any leadership team offsite or quarterly planning session. Work through each category, surface the items that belong in it, then apply the two-dimension prioritisation from Stage 5.

The question in Stage 2, what does the structure need to optimise for right now, is worth answering before any discussion of structural changes. Without a clear answer to that question, restructuring conversations tend to resolve around personal preferences and existing power dynamics rather than organisational need.
