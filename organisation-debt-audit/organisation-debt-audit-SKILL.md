# Organisation Debt Audit

This skill exists to help founders and senior leaders identify and prioritise the structural and operational liabilities their organisation has accumulated. Organisational debt compounds in the same way technical debt does: it accretes through decisions made for good reasons at the time that become constraints as the business grows, and it is almost never visible until the cost of carrying it exceeds the cost of addressing it.

The goal is not a restructuring plan. It is a prioritised map of the debt the organisation is currently carrying, with a clear assessment of which items are actively limiting growth and which can be deferred, so the founder can make informed decisions about where to invest repair effort.

Work through each stage in order. Every stage produces output before the next begins.

---

## Opening Orientation

Before beginning, establish the following with the founder in plain language:

This process examines the organisation's structure, processes, and role definitions as they currently exist, not as they are formally documented. In most growing businesses, these two things diverge significantly. The audit surfaces that divergence and assesses its cost.

The output is not a verdict on past decisions. Most organisational debt was created by reasonable people making reasonable decisions under the constraints of a different stage of the business. The audit examines the present situation, not the history of how it was created.

---

## Stage 1: Organisation Capture

Collect the raw material before any analysis begins. Ask the founder to describe:

- The current organisational structure: teams, reporting lines, and headcount
- The stage the business was at when the current structure was last deliberately designed
- The most significant changes in the business since that point: new products, new markets, new leadership, significant growth or contraction, strategic pivots
- Where the formal structure and the actual working structure diverge: who people actually go to, how decisions are actually made, which processes are consistently bypassed
- Where friction is highest: the decisions that take longest, the handoffs that most frequently break down, the roles that generate the most confusion

The gap between formal structure and actual working structure is where the most significant organisational debt is typically found. Do not accept the org chart at face value.

---

## Stage 2: First Principles Decomposition

Before mapping the debt, establish what the organisation's structure should be optimising for at this stage of the business.

Ask: what does this business need its organisational structure to do right now? Not in general terms, but specifically: what are the two or three things the structure must enable in order for the business to achieve its current objectives?

Common answers include: fast product iteration, consistent customer delivery, geographic expansion, sales velocity, regulatory compliance, or capital efficiency. The right answer is specific to this business at this moment.

Then ask: does the current structure optimise for those things, or does it optimise for something the business needed at an earlier stage? Structures built for a ten-person company frequently optimise for founder control and flexibility. Structures built for a fifty-person company frequently optimise for functional specialisation. Neither is right for a business that has grown beyond those stages.

Document what the structure should be optimising for in one sentence before proceeding.

---

## Stage 3: Debt Mapping**

Map the organisational debt across five categories. Work through each systematically.

**Structural debt**
Reporting lines, team boundaries, and spans of control that reflect the past rather than the present. Signs include: teams that formed around a product or initiative that no longer exists in its original form, reporting lines that made sense when the business was smaller but now create unnecessary distance between decision-makers and the work, spans of control that are too wide for effective management or too narrow to justify the management layer.

**Role debt**
Job titles and role definitions that no longer match what people actually do. Signs include: senior people doing work that should be delegated, titles that were given to retain someone but now misrepresent their actual scope, roles that have organically expanded into adjacent areas without formal recognition, and role boundaries that generate recurring conflict because they are genuinely ambiguous.

**Process debt**
Processes that were built for a different scale, a different team, or a different context and have become ritual rather than function. Signs include: approval chains longer than the decision risk warrants, meetings that exist because they were always scheduled rather than because they serve a current purpose, reporting cycles that produce information no one uses, and onboarding processes that reflect the company several versions ago.

**Decision debt**
Decision-making patterns that are too slow, too centralised, or too unclear for the current scale. Signs include: decisions that consistently escalate higher than they should, founder involvement in decisions that should be delegated, recurring situations where no one is sure who has authority to decide, and decisions that are made and then relitigated because the decision-making process lacks legitimacy.

**Cultural debt**
Norms, behaviours, and expectations that were functional at an earlier stage but are now limiting. Signs include: communication patterns that worked when everyone was in the same room but do not scale, hiring criteria that reflected the early team's profile rather than what the business now needs, performance expectations that are implicit rather than explicit, and values that are stated but not operationalised in any way that affects behaviour.

For each item of debt identified, note: which category it falls into, when it was approximately created, what business need it was serving at the time, and what it is currently costing in friction, speed, or quality.

---

## Stage 4: Bias Assessment

Assess the cognitive biases most likely to be sustaining the debt rather than prompting its resolution.

**Default scan**

*Sunk cost influence:* whether the organisation is maintaining structures, roles, or processes because of the investment made in creating them rather than because of their current value.

*Status quo bias:* whether the default is to leave existing structures intact unless there is an overwhelming case for change, setting a higher evidential bar for restructuring than the friction costs warrant.

*Authority bias:* whether structural debt is being sustained because the people most affected by it are not in a position to surface it to those with the authority to address it.

*Availability bias:* whether recent restructuring pain is making the founder reluctant to address current debt even where the cost of not addressing it is higher than the cost of the change.

*Overconfidence:* whether the founder is underestimating how much the current structure is costing relative to what they believe a better structure would deliver.

*In-group bias:* whether the people being consulted about structural issues are too close to the existing structure to assess it objectively.

**Contextual expansion**

*Endowment effect:* whether roles, teams, or processes are being preserved because they feel like existing assets rather than assessed on their current utility.

*Optimism bias:* whether the founder is assuming structural issues will resolve themselves as the team grows rather than compounding as it does.

For each bias assessed as present, identify which specific items of debt it is most likely sustaining and what it would take to overcome the bias sufficiently to act.

---

## Stage 5: Prioritisation

Prioritise the debt identified in Stage 3 across two dimensions.

**Growth impact:** how much is this item of debt currently limiting the organisation's ability to execute on its objectives? Rate as high, medium, or low.

**Remediation effort:** how much time, resource, and organisational disruption would addressing this item require? Rate as high, medium, or low.

The highest-priority items are those with high growth impact and low or medium remediation effort. These are the items where the return on repair investment is highest. Identify them explicitly.

Items with high growth impact and high remediation effort represent the significant restructuring decisions. These require deliberate planning and timing rather than immediate action, but they should be on the founder's roadmap rather than deferred indefinitely.

Items with low growth impact can be deprioritised regardless of remediation effort.

---

## Stage 6: The Reconstruction

For each high-priority debt item, produce a specific recommendation: what the current state is, what it is costing, what the improved state would look like, and what the path from current to improved state requires.

The recommendation should be specific enough to act on, not a general call for better processes or clearer roles. "Clarify decision rights" is not a recommendation. "Define explicitly who has authority to approve customer contract exceptions above £10,000, communicate this to the sales and legal teams, and remove the current escalation step to the COO for decisions below that threshold" is a recommendation.

For each recommendation, establish:

- What needs to happen
- Who needs to be involved
- What the likely points of resistance are and how to address them
- What the success indicator is: how the founder will know the debt has been resolved

---

## Stage 7: The Summary

Present this before the full staged analysis. It orients the founder to the shape of what they are about to engage with.

Use first principles thinking as the underlying architecture. Return to what the structure should be optimising for, identified in Stage 2, and state clearly whether the current structure is enabling or impeding that.

Structure the summary in three parts:

First, state what the organisation's structure needs to be doing at this stage of the business, in one plain sentence.

Second, state the single most significant item of organisational debt the audit has identified: the one that is most limiting growth and most feasibly addressed.

Third, state the recommended immediate action in one sentence.

The summary should be three sentences. The founder should understand the shape of the audit before entering the detail.

---

## Stage 8: Framing for Others

Help the founder communicate the audit findings to the relevant audience. The output is drafted, not described. Apply Toulmin's argumentation structure throughout: every claim accompanied by its grounds, the warrant connecting them, and acknowledgment of the conditions under which the claim would not hold.

**Leadership team**
The emphasis is on what the audit found, why it matters for the team's ability to execute, and what changes are being proposed and why. The leadership team needs to understand the reasoning behind structural changes, not just receive them as directives. Where changes affect roles or reporting lines, the framing should be honest about the reason rather than euphemistic.

**Board or investors**
The emphasis is on the relationship between organisational structure and growth trajectory, what the audit identified as the binding constraints, and what the plan is to address them. Boards and investors are more confident in founders who can diagnose structural limitations and address them proactively than in founders who attribute execution problems to market conditions.

**Personal record**
A complete record of the debt map, prioritisation, and recommendations for retrieval as the business continues to scale. Organisational debt that is deferred rather than resolved should be documented with the reasoning for deferral so it can be revisited rather than forgotten.

---

## Communication Principles

**Language calibration**
Translate findings into the specific language of this organisation. Name the actual teams, roles, and processes rather than describing them in the abstract. "The current approval process" is less useful than "the three-step approval chain for customer contract amendments that currently requires sign-off from you, the CFO, and the head of legal regardless of contract value."

**Observation separated from judgment**
The audit examines structure, not people. Where structural debt is embodied in a specific role or person, the framing should focus on the structural issue, not on the individual. This distinction is critical for maintaining trust during a conversation about potential restructuring.

**Progressive disclosure**
Lead with the two or three highest-priority items. Make the full debt map available but do not lead with a comprehensive list of everything that is suboptimal. A founder who leaves the audit feeling that everything is broken is less likely to act than one who leaves with a clear first step.

**The reconstruction as anchor**
The founder leaves with specific, actionable recommendations for the highest-priority items, not a catalogue of structural problems.
