# Founder Market Fit Review

A structured assessment of whether a founder's specific capabilities, risk profile, decision-making style, personal economics, and definition of success are genuinely well-matched to what their chosen market requires in order to win.

Founder-market fit is discussed in venture circles but almost never examined rigorously by founders themselves. The question is not whether the founder is passionate about the market. It is whether what this market fundamentally selects for, the capabilities, temperament, patience, and appetite for risk it requires of the people who win in it, matches what this specific founder naturally brings.

Mismatches at this level explain a significant proportion of founder attrition that gets attributed to market conditions or execution failure. The market was real, the product was reasonable, the team was capable, but the founder was persistently working against the grain of what the market required rather than with it.

This review produces an honest map of where the fit is strong, where it is workable with structural adjustment, and where there is a genuine constraint that needs either addressing or consciously accepting. It is most valuable run annually.

---

## Who it is for

Founders at any stage who want an honest structural assessment of their fit with the market they are operating in. It is particularly useful at inflection points: when something feels persistently hard in a way that execution improvement has not resolved, when a strategic decision requires the founder to assess what they are genuinely suited to, or when the business is evolving into a market that may require a different profile than the one that got it here.

This is not a motivational exercise. Run it for yourself first, with full honesty, before considering whether to share the output with anyone else.

## The eight stages

**1. Context Capture**
Collects the raw material: what the market is and how it works, the stage the business is at, what is working, what is consistently hard, and what the founder would change about the market if they could. That last question is particularly diagnostic: founders who wish their market moved faster, required less relationship-building, or rewarded different kinds of decisions are often experiencing a fit tension rather than an execution problem.

**2. First Principles Decomposition**
Establishes what this specific market fundamentally requires of the person leading a business in it, stripped of surface characteristics and tactics. Identifies the two or three fundamental requirements before the six-dimension assessment begins. This prevents the review from applying a generic framework rather than one calibrated to what this market actually selects for.

**3. Six-Dimension Fit Assessment**
The core of the review. Assesses fit across six dimensions, comparing what the market requires to what the founder brings, and identifying the gap between them as the fit finding.

The six dimensions are: domain knowledge and network depth (whether the founder has the insider access and established trust the market requires), financial runway and risk tolerance (whether the founder's personal financial situation and risk psychology are compatible with what the market requires in terms of patience and uncertainty), decision-making tempo (whether the founder's natural decision-making speed matches what the market rewards), relationship with ambiguity (whether the founder's tolerance for operating without confirmation matches what the market requires), personal definition of success (whether what the founder actually wants is compatible with what this market can realistically deliver), and energy and intrinsic motivation (whether the founder's energy for the work is driven by genuine engagement with the problem or by external validation, and whether the market's feedback cycle is compatible with what sustains them).

**4. Bias Assessment**
Assesses the cognitive biases most likely to be distorting the self-assessment. Runs a default scan of five biases: overconfidence, sunk cost influence, confirmation bias, social desirability bias, and optimism bias. Expands contextually to narrative bias and attribution bias where relevant. Social desirability bias receives particular attention in this review because founders are culturally expected to be confident in their market choice, which makes honest self-assessment structurally difficult.

**5. Fit Map**
Produces an explicit classification of the fit across all six dimensions: strong (a genuine advantage to be leveraged), workable (a gap that is addressable through team hire, structural adjustment, or conscious practice, with the adjustment specified), or constrained (a significant gap where the adjustment required is costly, slow, or requires persistent operation against the founder's natural grain, named explicitly as a known constraint).

**6. The Reconstruction**
For each dimension classified as workable or constrained, produces a specific recommendation and a structural hedge: a way of building the business so that the area of misfit is compensated for rather than ignored. These are not workarounds. They are structural responses to known constraints.

**7. The Summary**
Presented before the full staged analysis. Three sentences: what this market fundamentally requires of the person leading it, the single most important fit finding for the next twelve months, and the recommended action.

**8. Framing for Others**
More selective than in other skills in this library, because this review is primarily for the founder's own use. Where sharing is genuinely useful, applies Toulmin's argumentation structure throughout. Two audience frames: co-founder (as a mutual fit assessment that identifies complementary profiles and structural coverage) and coach or advisor (as a grounded basis for targeted development work rather than generic leadership coaching). Always includes a personal record for annual comparison.

## As a standalone framework

The six dimensions serve as an annual self-assessment checklist for any founder without Claude. For each dimension, ask: what does my market require here, what do I bring, and how large is the gap? Classify each gap as strong, workable, or constrained.

The most useful standalone question is the one from Stage 2: what does this market fundamentally require of the person leading a business in it, stripped of tactics and surface characteristics? Answering that question honestly before assessing your own fit against it is the move that makes the assessment meaningful rather than self-confirming.
