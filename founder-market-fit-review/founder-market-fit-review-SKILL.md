# Founder Market Fit Review

This skill exists to help founders examine whether they are genuinely well-matched to the market they are operating in. Not whether they are passionate about it. Not whether they have a credible founding story. Whether their specific capabilities, network, risk tolerance, decision-making tempo, relationship with ambiguity, personal economics, and definition of success are structurally compatible with what this specific market requires in order to win.

Mismatches at this level explain a significant proportion of founder attrition that gets attributed to market conditions or execution failure. The market was real, the product was reasonable, the team was capable, but the founder was fighting the grain of what the market required of them rather than working with it.

This review does not produce a verdict on whether the founder should continue. It produces an honest map of where the fit is strong, where it is workable with adjustment, and where there is a genuine mismatch that needs either addressing or consciously accepting as a known constraint.

Work through each stage in order. Every stage produces output before the next begins. This skill is most useful run with full honesty and without the filtering that occurs when the output will be shared with investors or co-founders. Run it for yourself first.

---

## Opening Orientation

Before beginning, establish the following in plain language:

This process examines fit, not ability. A founder can be highly capable and still be mismatched to a specific market. The review is not asking whether the founder is good enough. It is asking whether what this market requires to win matches what this founder naturally brings, and where the gaps are large enough to be addressed or accepted consciously.

The output is an honest assessment for the founder's own use. It may inform conversations with co-founders, coaches, or investors, but its primary value is in the clarity it creates for the founder themselves.

---

## Stage 1: Context Capture

Collect the raw material before any analysis begins. Ask the founder to describe:

- The market they are operating in: what it is, how it works, who the buyers are, how purchasing decisions are made, and what the competitive dynamics look like
- The stage the business is at: how long the founder has been operating in this market, what traction looks like, and what the next twelve months require
- What is working: where the founder feels they are operating naturally and effectively
- What is consistently hard: where the founder feels they are working against resistance rather than with momentum
- What they would change about the market if they could: this question surfaces where the market's fundamental characteristics are in tension with the founder's preferences

The last question is particularly useful. Founders who wish their market moved faster, had shorter sales cycles, required less relationship-building, or rewarded different kinds of decisions are often experiencing a fit tension rather than an execution problem.

---

## Stage 2: First Principles Decomposition

Before assessing fit across the six dimensions, establish what this specific market fundamentally requires of the person leading a business in it.

Strip away the surface characteristics of the market and ask: what does it take to win here, at the most fundamental level? Not the tactics. The underlying requirements.

Some markets reward speed of decision and tolerance for ambiguity above all else. Some reward depth of relationship and patience. Some reward technical credibility. Some reward the ability to navigate complex stakeholder environments. Some reward capital efficiency. Some reward the willingness to take concentrated bets.

Identify the two or three fundamental requirements of this market before assessing the founder's fit against them. This prevents the assessment from becoming generic rather than specific to what this market actually selects for.

---

## Stage 3: Six-Dimension Fit Assessment

Assess the founder's fit against the market across six dimensions. For each dimension, the assessment has two parts: what the market requires, and what the founder brings. The gap between them is the fit finding.

**Dimension 1: Domain knowledge and network depth**
What does this market require in terms of domain expertise and existing relationships? Some markets can be entered by a capable generalist with a strong team. Others require deep insider knowledge and established trust networks that take years to build. Assess honestly: does the founder have the domain knowledge and network the market requires, or are they compensating for gaps through speed, capital, or team hire? Compensation is not inherently wrong, but it should be conscious and costed.

**Dimension 2: Financial runway and risk tolerance**
What is the capital intensity of this market and the likely time to meaningful revenue? Compare this honestly to the founder's personal financial runway, their household's tolerance for income uncertainty, and their psychological relationship with financial risk. A founder operating at the edge of their financial tolerance makes systematically worse decisions: they over-index on short-term revenue at the cost of strategic positioning, they cannot hold firm in negotiations, and they take investors they should not take. Assess the fit between what this market requires in terms of financial patience and what the founder can actually sustain.

**Dimension 3: Decision-making tempo**
How fast does this market require decisions to be made? Some markets reward rapid iteration and tolerance for reversible mistakes. Others require careful deliberation and the ability to hold a position over a long horizon. Assess the founder's natural decision-making tempo. Founders who are natural fast-decision-makers in slow markets tend toward premature pivoting. Founders who are natural deliberators in fast markets tend toward missed windows. Neither is wrong. The question is fit.

**Dimension 4: Relationship with ambiguity**
How much ambiguity does this market require the founder to hold, and for how long? Early-stage markets with no established playbook require high tolerance for operating without confirmation that the path is correct. More established markets with clearer competitive dynamics require less ambiguity tolerance but more patience with slow-moving processes. Assess honestly where the founder sits on this spectrum and whether it matches what the market requires.

**Dimension 5: Personal definition of success**
What does the founder actually want from this business, in specific terms: financial outcome, autonomy, impact, recognition, intellectual engagement, or something else? Now ask: is what this market can realistically deliver compatible with what the founder wants? A founder building in a market that rewards patient capital accumulation over twenty years who needs a liquidity event within five years has a structural misalignment between their definition of success and what the market will provide. This misalignment tends to produce decisions that optimise for the exit rather than for the business, which typically produces neither.

**Dimension 6: Energy and intrinsic motivation**
Is the founder's energy for this work intrinsic, driven by genuine engagement with the problem, the market, and the work itself, or is it contingent on external validation: investor interest, revenue growth, press coverage, or peer recognition? Intrinsic motivation sustains through the periods when external signals are absent or negative. Contingent motivation tends to collapse precisely when the business most needs steady leadership. Assess honestly which is true, and whether the market's feedback cycle, how quickly it provides validation and of what kind, is compatible with what sustains this founder's energy.

---

## Stage 4: Bias Assessment

Assess the cognitive biases most likely to be distorting the founder's self-assessment across the six dimensions.

**Default scan**

*Overconfidence:* whether the founder is assessing their capabilities and fit more favourably than the evidence supports.

*Sunk cost influence:* whether prior investment in this market, time, capital, reputation, is making the founder more reluctant to acknowledge genuine mismatches than the evidence warrants.

*Confirmation bias:* whether the founder is interpreting ambiguous evidence about their fit in the direction that supports continuing rather than examining it neutrally.

*Social desirability bias:* whether the founder is answering the fit questions in the way they think they should answer them rather than honestly. This bias is particularly strong in this exercise because founders are culturally expected to be confident in their market choice.

*Optimism bias:* whether the assessment of what the market requires is more favourable than an objective reading of comparable businesses in this market would suggest.

**Contextual expansion**

*Narrative bias:* whether the founder's founding story has become so rehearsed that they are no longer able to assess the fit questions freshly rather than through the lens of the story they tell about themselves and the business.

*Attribution bias:* whether the founder is attributing misfit signals, persistent friction, low energy, resistance to the work, to external factors rather than to genuine fit questions.

For each bias assessed as present, identify which dimension of the fit assessment it is most likely distorting and in which direction.

---

## Stage 5: Fit Map

Produce an explicit fit map across the six dimensions.

For each dimension, classify the fit as:

**Strong:** the founder's profile matches or exceeds what this market requires. This is a genuine advantage and should be leveraged consciously.

**Workable:** there is a gap between what the market requires and what the founder naturally brings, but it is addressable through team hire, structural adjustment, or conscious practice. Specify what the adjustment is.

**Constrained:** the gap is significant and the adjustment required is either very costly, very slow, or would require the founder to operate persistently against their natural grain. This is not a reason to stop, but it is a constraint that should be named explicitly and factored into decisions about team composition, investor profile, and the pace and nature of growth.

The fit map is the core output of the review. It should be honest enough to be useful and specific enough to be actionable.

---

## Stage 6: The Reconstruction

For each dimension classified as Workable or Constrained, produce a specific recommendation: what the gap is, what addressing it would require, and what the realistic timeline and cost of that adjustment is.

For Constrained dimensions, also produce a structural hedge: a way of building the business so that the area of misfit is compensated for rather than ignored. A founder who is a natural deliberator in a fast market can build in decision-forcing mechanisms. A founder operating beyond their financial comfort can structure personal liquidity earlier. A founder whose energy is contingent on external validation can build in shorter feedback loops. These are not workarounds. They are structural responses to known constraints.

---

## Stage 7: The Summary

Present this before the full staged analysis.

Use first principles thinking as the underlying architecture. Return to the fundamental requirements of the market identified in Stage 2 and state clearly where the fit is strong, where it requires active management, and where the most significant constraint lies.

Structure the summary in three parts:

First, state what this market fundamentally requires of the person leading it, in one plain sentence.

Second, state the single most important fit finding: the dimension where the assessment is most consequential for the founder's decisions over the next twelve months.

Third, state the recommended action in one sentence: what the founder should do differently, build structurally, or consciously accept as a known constraint.

The summary should be three sentences. The founder should understand the shape of the review before entering the detail.

---

## Stage 8: Framing for Others

This review is primarily for the founder's own use. The framing for others stage is therefore more selective than in other skills. Apply it only where sharing is genuinely useful.

Apply Toulmin's argumentation structure throughout any output shared: every claim accompanied by its grounds, the warrant connecting them, and acknowledgment of the conditions under which the claim would not hold.

**Co-founder**
Where a co-founder relationship exists, sharing the fit map creates an opportunity to identify complementary fit profiles and structural coverage of each other's constrained dimensions. Frame the conversation as a mutual assessment rather than a one-sided disclosure.

**Coach or advisor**
Where the founder works with a coach or trusted advisor, the fit map provides a grounded basis for the relationship's focus. Rather than working on generic leadership development, the work can be targeted at the specific dimensions where the fit is constrained.

**Personal record**
Document the full fit map and reconstruction annually. The value of the review compounds over time: comparing this year's assessment to last year's shows which dimensions have strengthened, which adjustments have been made and whether they have worked, and whether new mismatches have emerged as the market or the business has evolved.

---

## Communication Principles

**Honesty as the primary requirement**
This review has no value if the founder answers the fit questions in the way they think they should answer them. The communication principle here is different from the other skills in this library: the primary audience is the founder themselves, and the primary requirement is honesty rather than persuasion. Apply that standard throughout.

**Observation separated from judgment**
The review examines fit, not worth. A constrained fit finding is not a verdict on the founder's capability or character. It is a structural observation about the relationship between what they bring and what this market requires. Maintain that distinction throughout.

**Progressive disclosure**
Lead with the fit map. Make the full assessment available but give the founder the summary before the detail. A founder reading six pages of analysis before knowing the headline finding is more likely to interpret the detail through anxiety than through clarity.

**The reconstruction as anchor**
The founder leaves with a specific set of structural adjustments for their constrained dimensions, not a catalogue of mismatches. The register is generative and forward-facing.
