# Board Reporting Brief

A structured preparation process for board meetings that covers what decisions need to be made versus reported, the likely concerns of each board member, agenda sequencing, explicit asks, and disagreement preparation. The output is a brief for the founder's own use in the forty-eight hours before the meeting.

Most founders under-prepare for board meetings in a specific way. They prepare the content but not the conversation. They update metrics, brief investors on progress, and build slides, but they do not prepare for the decisions the board actually needs to make, the concerns that will surface but not be stated directly, the dynamics between board members that will shape how information lands, or the specific asks they need to get out of the meeting in order to move forward. The result is board meetings that consume significant preparation time and produce little strategic value.

This skill addresses the preparation gap, not the content gap.

---

## Who it is for

Founders and senior leaders preparing for any formal board or investor reporting session. It is most valuable when the meeting involves decisions as well as reporting, when there is likely to be disagreement or difficult news, or when the founder needs to get something specific out of the meeting and is not sure how to ask for it effectively.

---

## The ten stages

**1. Meeting Context Capture**
Collects the raw material: when the meeting is, who will be in the room, what has happened since the last meeting, what the current state of the business looks like on the metrics the board tracks, what decisions need to be made versus reported, what the founder needs to get out of the meeting, and what the founder is most concerned about. The founder's concerns are treated as data pointing to where preparation effort should be concentrated.

**2. First Principles Decomposition**
Establishes what this specific meeting needs to accomplish at its most fundamental level: if the founder could leave with one thing, what would it be? Then asks whether the current plan for the meeting is structured to achieve that outcome or structured around reporting convention. Most board meetings are structured for information transfer rather than for the conversations that move the business forward. This stage identifies the gap.

**3. Decision vs Reporting Separation**
Separates every agenda item into decisions (where the board needs to make, ratify, or provide input on a choice, requiring structured framing and deliberation time) and reporting (where the board is being updated, requiring clarity and honesty but not deliberation time). For each decision item, prepares the Toulmin argumentation structure in advance: claim, grounds, warrant, backing, qualifier, and rebuttal. Arriving at a board meeting with this structure prepared for each decision item means the founder cannot be caught without an answer when challenged.

**4. Board Member Assessment**
For each person who will be in the room, prepares an assessment of their primary interest and lens, their likely concerns about the current period, their likely unstated concerns, what they need to leave the meeting with, and their communication style. Unstated concerns shape how board members receive information even when they do not voice them. This assessment is for the founder's preparation only.

**5. Bias Assessment**
Assesses the cognitive biases most likely to be shaping how the founder is preparing and how they are likely to perform in the meeting. Runs a default scan of five biases: confirmation bias, authority bias, social desirability bias, overconfidence, and loss aversion. Expands contextually to in-group bias and anchoring. Each bias identified is mapped to where it is affecting the preparation and what the corrective adjustment is.

**6. Agenda Design**
Designs the meeting agenda based on the analysis rather than on convention. Three structural principles: decisions scheduled first at the point of highest attention, time allocated to importance rather than to seniority of function, and explicit time protected for the one conversation the meeting most needs to have. For each agenda item, specifies the category, time allocated, who is leading it, and the desired outcome.

**7. The Asks**
Identifies explicitly what the founder needs to ask for and how to ask for it. Most founders arrive hoping that what they need will be offered. It frequently is not. For each ask, specifies what it is as precisely as possible, which board member it is directed at, when in the agenda it should be made, the grounds for asking, and what the founder will do if the answer is yes or no. Asks without a prepared response to a no are not ready to make.

**8. Disagreement Preparation**
Identifies the points in the meeting where disagreement is most likely to arise and prepares for them specifically. For each likely point of disagreement, prepares the founder's position using Toulmin structure and identifies what a productive resolution looks like and what an unproductive version of the same conversation looks like. Disagreement in a board meeting is not a failure. It is frequently where the most valuable thinking happens. The preparation is designed to ensure the founder can engage with it constructively rather than defensively.

**9. The Preparation Brief**
Compiles all preceding stage outputs into a single document for the founder's use in the forty-eight hours before the meeting: the meeting's primary purpose, the structured agenda, the board member assessment, the bias assessment and corrective adjustments, the explicit asks, and the disagreement preparation. Honest rather than polished. Its value is in the clarity it creates before the meeting.

**10. Framing for Others**
Addresses the communication that comes out of the meeting rather than the preparation that goes into it. Applies Toulmin's argumentation structure throughout. Three outputs: a post-meeting summary to the board (sent within twenty-four hours, establishing shared understanding of what was decided and who is responsible for what), a post-meeting communication to the leadership team where the meeting has implications for their priorities, and a personal record for improving preparation over time.

## As a standalone framework

The three-question framework from Stages 2 and 3 is the most useful standalone tool in this skill. Before any board meeting, ask: what do I most need to leave this meeting with, which agenda items are decisions versus reporting, and what am I going to ask for explicitly? Those three questions alone will improve the quality of most board meetings.

The Toulmin structure for decision items in Stage 3 applies to any situation where the founder needs to present a recommendation to a group that will challenge it: claim, grounds, warrant, backing, qualifier, rebuttal. Building this for every major recommendation before a board meeting means the founder cannot be caught without an answer.
