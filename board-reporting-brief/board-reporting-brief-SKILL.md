# Board Reporting Brief

This skill exists to help founders prepare for board meetings in the way that actually determines whether the meeting is useful: not by building a better deck, but by thinking clearly about what the meeting needs to accomplish, what each person in the room needs to leave with, and how to structure the conversation so the most important things get addressed rather than crowded out by reporting.

Most founders under-prepare for board meetings in a specific way. They prepare the content but not the conversation. They update metrics, brief investors on progress, and build slides, but they do not prepare for the decisions the board actually needs to make, the concerns that will surface but not be stated directly, the dynamics between board members that will shape how information lands, or the specific asks they need to get out of the meeting in order to move forward. The result is board meetings that consume significant preparation time and produce little strategic value.

This skill produces a preparation brief for use in the forty-eight hours before the meeting.

Work through each stage in order. Every stage produces output before the next begins.

---

## Opening Orientation

Before beginning, establish the following in plain language:

This process prepares the founder for the conversation the meeting will actually be, not the meeting they plan to run. Board members arrive with their own contexts, concerns, and agendas. Understanding those in advance and designing the meeting to accommodate them productively is the work that determines whether the meeting is useful. Building a better deck is not.

The output is a preparation brief, not a board pack. The board pack communicates to the board. The preparation brief is for the founder's own use.

---

## Stage 1: Meeting Context Capture

Collect the raw material before any preparation begins. Ask the founder to describe:

- When the meeting is and how long it is scheduled for
- Who will be in the room: names, roles, and how long each has been on the board
- What has happened in the business since the last board meeting: the significant developments, positive and negative
- What the current state of the business is on the metrics the board tracks
- What decisions need to be made in this meeting, as distinct from what is being reported for information
- What the founder needs to get out of this meeting: approvals, introductions, guidance, or support
- What the founder is most concerned about in terms of how the meeting will go

That last point is important. The founder's concerns about the meeting are data: they point to where preparation effort should be concentrated.

---

## Stage 2: First Principles Decomposition

Before preparing content or agenda, establish what this specific board meeting needs to accomplish at the most fundamental level.

Ask: if the founder could leave this meeting with one thing, what would it be? Not the formal agenda items, but the outcome that would make the meeting genuinely valuable. An approval, a shift in a board member's perspective, a specific piece of guidance, a decision that has been blocking progress, or simply a shared understanding of where the business is and what it needs.

Then ask: is the current plan for the meeting structured to achieve that outcome, or is it structured around reporting convention? Most board meetings are structured around convention: updates by function, metrics review, any other business. That structure is designed for information transfer, not for the conversations that actually move the business forward. The preparation brief should identify where the conventional structure serves the meeting's real purpose and where it does not.

---

## Stage 3: Decision vs Reporting Separation

Separate every agenda item into one of two categories.

**Decisions:** items where the board needs to make, ratify, or provide input on a specific choice. These require structured framing: the decision to be made, the options considered, the founder's recommendation, and the reasoning. They require adequate time and should be scheduled when the board's attention is highest.

**Reporting:** items where the board is being updated on progress, performance, or status. These require clarity and honesty but do not require deliberation time. They can be pre-read or handled efficiently in the meeting.

Most board meetings allocate more time to reporting than to decisions and run out of time for the conversations that matter. The preparation brief should identify where time is being misallocated and propose a resequencing where necessary.

For each decision item, prepare the Toulmin argumentation structure in advance: the claim (the recommended decision), the grounds (the evidence supporting it), the warrant (the reasoning connecting the evidence to the recommendation), the backing (the deeper principles or data that support the warrant), the qualifier (the conditions under which the recommendation would be different), and the rebuttal (the strongest objection to the recommendation and the founder's response to it). Arriving at a board meeting with this structure prepared for each decision item prevents the founder from being caught without an answer when challenged.

---

## Stage 4: Board Member Assessment

For each person who will be in the room, prepare an assessment of what they are likely to bring to this specific meeting.

For each board member, establish:

**Primary interest and lens:** what does this board member most care about and most consistently focus on? Financial performance, strategic positioning, team quality, market dynamics, risk management, or something else specific to their background.

**Likely concerns about the current period:** based on what has happened since the last meeting, what is this board member most likely to raise, challenge, or want to explore? These concerns may or may not be stated explicitly in advance.

**Likely unstated concerns:** what might this board member be thinking or worried about that they are unlikely to raise directly? Unstated concerns shape how board members receive information even when they do not voice them. A board member concerned about the founder's judgment will receive a revenue miss differently from one who is not.

**What they need to leave with:** what does this board member need from this meeting in order to feel that their involvement is well-directed? This is distinct from what the founder needs from them.

**Communication style:** how does this board member prefer to receive information? Some board members engage best with data presented upfront. Others engage best with narrative before numbers. Some want to challenge. Others want to build. Calibrating to each individual's style is not manipulation: it is effective communication.

This assessment is for the founder's preparation only. It should not be shared with board members.

---

## Stage 5: Bias Assessment

Assess the cognitive biases most likely to be shaping how the founder is preparing for this meeting and how they are likely to perform in it.

**Default scan**

*Confirmation bias:* whether the founder is preparing to present information in a way that emphasises evidence supporting the current strategy and underweights evidence that challenges it.

*Authority bias:* whether the founder is deferring to the likely views of the most senior or most assertive board member in a way that pre-empts their own judgment.

*Social desirability bias:* whether the founder is preparing to present a version of the business's situation that is shaped by what they believe the board wants to hear rather than what the board needs to hear.

*Overconfidence:* whether the founder's preparation assumes the meeting will go more smoothly than board meetings typically do, leading to under-preparation for challenge or disagreement.

*Loss aversion:* whether concerns about how the board will receive negative news are leading the founder to soften, bury, or delay presenting information the board needs.

**Contextual expansion**

*In-group bias:* whether the founder is over-preparing for the board members they have the strongest relationship with and under-preparing for those they are less comfortable with.

*Anchoring:* whether a particularly strong reaction from a board member in a previous meeting is shaping how the founder is framing this one in a way that may not be appropriate to the current situation.

For each bias assessed as present, identify specifically where it is affecting the preparation and what the corrective adjustment is.

---

## Stage 6: Agenda Design

Design the meeting agenda based on the preceding analysis, not on convention.

The agenda should be structured around three principles:

**Decisions first, at the point of highest attention.** If there are decisions to be made, schedule them early. Board members' attention and appetite for deliberation decline through a meeting. Scheduling important decisions for the end of a two-hour meeting is a structural error.

**Time allocated to importance, not to seniority of function.** The time given to each agenda item should reflect its strategic importance, not the seniority of the person presenting it. A five-minute CFO update on a stable financial position should not crowd out a thirty-minute conversation about a critical strategic question.

**Explicit time for the conversation that matters most.** Based on the preparation in Stages 2 and 4, identify the one conversation this meeting most needs to have and protect time for it explicitly. If that conversation is not on the agenda, it will not happen.

For each agenda item, specify: the item, the category (decision or reporting), the time allocated, who is leading it, and what the desired outcome is.

---

## Stage 7: The Asks

Identify explicitly what the founder needs to ask for in this meeting and how to ask for it.

Most founders arrive at board meetings hoping that what they need will be offered. It frequently is not, because board members do not know what the founder needs unless they are asked directly. Vague asks produce vague responses. Specific asks produce useful responses or useful refusals.

For each ask the founder needs to make:

- State the ask as precisely as possible
- Identify which board member or members the ask is directed at
- Identify when in the agenda the ask should be made
- Prepare the grounds for the ask: why is this needed, what would it enable, and why is now the right time to ask for it
- Identify what a yes looks like, what a no looks like, and what the founder will do in each case

Asks that do not have a prepared response to a no are not ready to make.

---

## Stage 8: Disagreement Preparation

Identify the points in the meeting where disagreement is most likely to arise and prepare for them specifically.

For each likely point of disagreement:

- State what the disagreement is likely to be about
- Identify which board member or members are most likely to raise it
- Prepare the founder's position using Toulmin structure: claim, grounds, warrant, backing, qualifier, rebuttal
- Identify what a productive resolution of the disagreement looks like and what the founder needs to do to get there
- Identify what an unproductive version of the same conversation looks like and what the founder needs to avoid doing to prevent it

Disagreement in a board meeting is not a failure of the meeting. It is frequently where the most valuable thinking happens. The preparation is not designed to avoid disagreement. It is designed to ensure the founder can engage with it constructively rather than defensively.

---

## Stage 9: The Preparation Brief

Compile the outputs of all preceding stages into a single preparation document for the founder's use in the forty-eight hours before the meeting.

The brief should cover:

- The meeting's primary purpose: what the founder most needs to leave with
- The agenda, with decision items and their Toulmin-structured framing
- The board member assessment: what each person is likely to bring and what they need to leave with
- The bias assessment and corrective adjustments
- The explicit asks, prepared and ready to make
- The disagreement preparation: the likely points of challenge and the founder's prepared position on each

The brief is for the founder's own use. It should be honest rather than polished. Its value is in the clarity it creates before the meeting, not in how it reads.

---

## Stage 10: Framing for Others

The primary output of this skill is for the founder's own preparation. The framing for others stage addresses the communication that comes out of the meeting rather than the preparation that goes into it.

Apply Toulmin's argumentation structure throughout: every claim accompanied by its grounds, the warrant connecting them, and acknowledgment of the conditions under which the claim would not hold.

**Post-meeting communication to the board**
Where the meeting produces decisions, commitments, or action items, a clear written summary sent within twenty-four hours establishes shared understanding and prevents the retrospective rewriting of what was decided. The summary should state what was decided, what the grounds were, and who is responsible for what by when.

**Post-meeting communication to the leadership team**
Where the board meeting has implications for the team's priorities, direction, or understanding of the business's situation, a summary communication to the leadership team prevents the board meeting from becoming a black box. The summary should share what can be shared without breaching the confidentiality appropriate to board proceedings.

**Personal record**
Document what was prepared, what was asked, what happened, and what was different from what was anticipated. This record is the basis for improving board meeting preparation over time. Patterns in what consistently surprises the founder in board meetings, what consistently does not land as intended, and what consistently produces useful outcomes are the most valuable preparation intelligence available.

---

## Communication Principles

**Preparation is not performance**
The preparation brief is designed to help the founder think clearly before the meeting, not to script the meeting. The goal is that the founder arrives with enough clarity about what the meeting needs to accomplish and enough preparation for the likely challenges that they can engage flexibly and honestly rather than defensively.

**Honesty as the primary requirement**
The bias assessment in Stage 5 exists because board meetings are high-social-stakes environments that systematically produce distorted communication. Founders who tell boards what they want to hear rather than what they need to hear destroy the board's ability to be useful. The preparation brief should reinforce honesty rather than optimise for comfort.

**Progressive disclosure**
Lead with the primary purpose and the decisions to be made. Everything else is context that serves those. A board that spends the majority of the meeting on reporting and runs out of time for decisions is a board that is not being well-led.

**The conversation as the output**
The board pack is an input to the meeting. The preparation brief is a tool for running the meeting well. The output is the conversation that happens and the decisions, commitments, and clarity it produces.
