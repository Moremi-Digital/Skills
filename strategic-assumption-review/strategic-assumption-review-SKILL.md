# Strategic Assumption Review

This skill exists to surface what a founder is betting on before they bet. Every strategic initiative rests on a stack of assumptions. Most founders cannot articulate what those assumptions are until one of them fails. This skill makes the stack visible and actionable before execution begins.

It is prospective. It operates before commitment, not after. The companion skill for retrospective examination is the Decision Audit.

Work through each stage in order. Every stage produces output before the next begins.

---

## Opening Orientation

Before beginning, establish the following with the founder in plain language:

This process does not evaluate whether the initiative is a good idea. It maps what the initiative is betting on so the founder can make an informed decision about whether those bets are ones they want to take, which ones to test before committing, and which ones are acceptable to take on faith given the cost of testing relative to the cost of being wrong.

The output is not a recommendation to proceed or stop. It is a prioritised map of the assumption stack with a testing plan for the highest-risk items.

---

## Stage 1: Initiative Capture

Collect the raw material before any analysis begins. Ask the founder to describe:

- What the initiative is, stated as precisely as possible
- What it is intended to achieve, the stated objective
- What success looks like in concrete, measurable terms at six months, one year, and three years
- What resources are being committed: capital, time, people, opportunity cost
- What the reversibility of the commitment is: at what point does this become difficult or impossible to unwind
- What has already been decided and what is still open

The reversibility assessment is critical. Irreversible or high-cost-to-reverse commitments warrant a more thorough assumption review than easily reversible ones. Establish this clearly before proceeding.

---

## Stage 2: First Principles Decomposition

Before mapping assumptions, strip the initiative back to its most fundamental components. This prevents the review from operating within the founder's existing frame rather than examining the frame itself.

Ask: what is the most basic problem or opportunity this initiative is a response to? Strip away the specific solution, the market context, the competitive rationale, and the timing logic. What remains at the root?

Then ask: is this initiative the most direct response to that root problem or opportunity, or is it a response to a more comfortable or familiar version of it? Founders frequently design initiatives that address a symptom or a proxy rather than the underlying condition. Surfacing this gap at the outset reorients the entire assumption review.

Document the root problem or opportunity in one sentence before proceeding to assumption mapping.

---

## Stage 3: Assumption Extraction

Extract every assumption embedded in the initiative. Do not filter at this stage. The goal is completeness before classification.

Assumptions typically cluster in six categories. Work through each systematically:

**Market assumptions:** what the initiative assumes about the size, growth, accessibility, and behaviour of the target market.

**Customer assumptions:** what it assumes about who the customer is, what they want, how they make decisions, how much they will pay, and how they will be reached.

**Competitive assumptions:** what it assumes about competitor behaviour, competitive response, and the durability of any advantage the initiative creates.

**Capability assumptions:** what it assumes about the organisation's ability to execute: skills, capacity, speed, and institutional knowledge.

**Financial assumptions:** what it assumes about unit economics, payback periods, capital requirements, and the relationship between growth and cash consumption.

**Timing assumptions:** what it assumes about market readiness, the window of opportunity, and the sequencing of dependencies.

List every assumption surfaced. Do not evaluate yet.

---

## Stage 4: Assumption Classification

Classify each assumption across two dimensions.

**Load-bearing weight:** how much does the initiative depend on this assumption being correct?

- Critical: the initiative fails or requires fundamental redesign if this assumption is wrong
- Significant: the initiative is materially impaired but not fatal if this assumption is wrong
- Peripheral: the initiative holds substantially intact if this assumption is wrong

**Testability:** how difficult is it to test this assumption before committing to the full initiative?

- High testability: can be tested quickly and cheaply with existing resources
- Medium testability: requires moderate time or resource investment to test
- Low testability: difficult or expensive to test before commitment, or only verifiable through full execution

The highest-priority assumptions are those classified as Critical and either High or Medium testability. These are the assumptions most worth testing and most feasibly tested before commitment. Identify them explicitly.

Assumptions classified as Critical and Low testability represent the irreducible bets in the initiative. These should be named clearly and accepted consciously rather than ignored.

---

## Stage 5: Bias Assessment

Assess the cognitive biases most likely to have shaped the assumption stack. The goal is to identify where the assumptions may be systematically distorted rather than randomly uncertain.

**Default scan: run on every review**

*Confirmation bias:* whether the assumption set disproportionately reflects evidence that supports the initiative and underweights evidence that challenges it.

*Overconfidence:* whether the confidence level assigned to critical assumptions is proportionate to the evidence base or exceeds it.

*Planning fallacy:* whether timing and resource assumptions are systematically optimistic relative to comparable initiatives.

*Availability bias:* whether recent successes or failures in adjacent areas are distorting the probability assessment of outcomes here.

*Sunk cost influence:* whether prior investment in related work is making the founder more committed to this initiative than the current evidence warrants.

*In-group bias:* whether the people consulted in developing the initiative were too similar in perspective to provide genuine challenge to the assumption set.

**Contextual expansion**

Assess additionally where the specific initiative warrants it:

*Bandwagon effect:* whether the initiative is partly driven by what competitors or the market are doing rather than independent assessment of what is right for this business.

*Anchoring:* whether an early piece of data, a competitor's pricing, a market size figure, an investor comment, has become a reference point that is shaping assumptions beyond its evidential weight.

*Optimism bias:* whether customer adoption, revenue ramp, and competitive response assumptions consistently reflect best-case rather than base-case scenarios.

*Authority bias:* whether assumptions have been accepted because they came from a respected investor, advisor, or industry figure rather than because they are independently supported.

For each bias assessed as present, identify which specific assumptions it has most likely distorted and in which direction.

---

## Stage 6: Minimum Viable Tests

For each assumption classified as Critical with High or Medium testability, design a minimum viable test: the smallest, cheapest, fastest experiment that would meaningfully update the founder's confidence in that assumption.

For each test, specify:

- What the test is
- What it would cost in time and resource
- What result would increase confidence in the assumption
- What result would decrease confidence and by how much
- What the founder would do differently based on each outcome

Tests that do not have a specified action based on each outcome are not tests. They are research. Distinguish between the two.

Do not design tests for peripheral assumptions. The goal is to concentrate testing resource on the assumptions that most affect the initiative's viability.

---

## Stage 7: The Irreducible Bets

Name the assumptions classified as Critical and Low testability explicitly. These are the bets the founder is taking without the ability to test them first.

For each irreducible bet, establish:

- Why it is low testability: what makes it difficult or impossible to verify before commitment
- What the downside looks like if the assumption proves wrong
- Whether there is a structural hedge available: a way to design the initiative so that if this assumption fails, the damage is contained
- Whether the founder is consciously accepting this bet or has not yet fully reckoned with it

Irreducible bets are not a reason to stop. They are the nature of strategic commitment. The goal is that the founder enters the initiative knowing exactly what they are betting on and having consciously accepted each bet rather than discovered it afterward.

---

## Stage 8: The Summary

Present this before the full staged analysis. It orients the founder to the shape of what they are about to engage with.

Use first principles thinking as the underlying architecture. Return to the root problem or opportunity identified in Stage 2 and state whether the initiative addresses it directly or at a remove.

Structure the summary in three parts:

First, state the root problem or opportunity the initiative is responding to, in one plain sentence.

Second, state the single most important finding from the assumption review: the assumption that most needs attention before the founder proceeds, whether because it is critical and testable or because it is critical and irreducible and has not been consciously accepted.

Third, state the recommended next action in one sentence: the one test to run, the one bet to consciously accept, or the one redesign to consider.

The summary should be three sentences. The founder should understand the shape of the review before entering the detail.

---

## Stage 9: Framing for Others

Help the founder communicate the assumption review and its findings to the relevant audience. The output is drafted, not described. Apply Toulmin's argumentation structure throughout: every claim should be accompanied by the grounds on which it rests, the warrant connecting grounds to claim, and an acknowledgment of the conditions under which the claim would not hold.

**Co-founder or leadership team**
The emphasis is on the assumption stack itself and the testing plan. The team needs to understand what the initiative is betting on, which assumptions they are being asked to help test, and what the decision criteria are for proceeding after testing. Frame the irreducible bets explicitly so the team can consciously commit to them rather than discovering them mid-execution.

**Board or investors**
The emphasis is on the rigour of the pre-commitment process, the highest-risk assumptions and how they are being addressed, and the conditions under which the initiative would be redesigned or stopped. Boards and investors are more confident in founders who can articulate what they do not yet know than in founders who present false certainty.

**Personal record**
A complete written record of the assumption stack, classifications, test designs, and irreducible bets for retrieval if the initiative encounters difficulty. This is the document that allows the founder to distinguish between an execution failure and an assumption failure when things go wrong.

---

## Communication Principles

These apply throughout every stage.

**Language calibration**
Translate findings into the specific language of this initiative. "Overconfidence bias is present" is less useful than "the customer adoption curve in this model assumes a conversion rate three times higher than your current product achieves, and there is no evidence in the review for why this initiative would perform differently." Specificity is what makes a finding actionable.

**Observation separated from judgment**
The review examines assumptions and their evidential basis, not the quality of the founder's thinking. Frame output as a structured examination conducted alongside the founder, not an evaluation of their judgment.

**Progressive disclosure**
Lead with the two or three assumptions with the highest implications. Make the full stack available but do not lead with volume. A review that surfaces twenty critical assumptions simultaneously is accurate but paralysing.

**The testing plan as anchor**
The founder leaves with a specific, actionable testing plan for the highest-risk testable assumptions and a clear statement of the irreducible bets they are consciously accepting. Not a list of concerns.
