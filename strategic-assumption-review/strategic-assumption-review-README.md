# Strategic Assumption Review

A structured pre-commitment review that surfaces every assumption embedded in a strategic initiative before resources are committed.

Every strategic bet rests on a stack of assumptions. The problem is not that founders make assumptions: all decisions under uncertainty require them. The problem is that most founders cannot articulate what their assumptions are until one of them fails. By then, the cost of being wrong has already been paid.

This skill makes the assumption stack visible before execution begins, classifies each assumption by how much the initiative depends on it and how feasibly it can be tested, designs minimum viable tests for the highest-risk items, and names the irreducible bets the founder is taking consciously rather than discovering mid-execution.

It is prospective. It operates before commitment. The companion skill for examining decisions after the fact is the Decision Audit.

---

## Who it is for

Founders and senior leaders preparing to commit meaningful resources to a strategic initiative: a new market entry, a product pivot, a pricing change, a significant hire, a partnership, a fundraise, or any move where the cost of a wrong assumption is material.

---

## The nine stages

**1. Initiative Capture**
Collects the raw material: what the initiative is, what it is trying to achieve, what success looks like in concrete terms, what resources are being committed, and how reversible the commitment is. Reversibility determines the appropriate depth of review before proceeding.

**2. First Principles Decomposition**
Strips the initiative back to the root problem or opportunity it is responding to, before any solution logic is applied. This prevents the review from operating within the founder's existing frame rather than examining the frame itself. Founders frequently design initiatives that address a symptom or a proxy rather than the underlying condition. This stage surfaces that gap before the assumption mapping begins.

**3. Assumption Extraction**
Extracts every assumption embedded in the initiative across six categories: market, customer, competitive, capability, financial, and timing. No filtering at this stage. The goal is completeness before classification.

**4. Assumption Classification**
Classifies each assumption across two dimensions: how load-bearing it is (critical, significant, or peripheral) and how testable it is before commitment (high, medium, or low). The highest-priority assumptions are those that are both critical and testable. These are the assumptions most worth testing and most feasibly tested before commitment. Critical assumptions that are low testability are named explicitly as the irreducible bets in the initiative.

**5. Bias Assessment**
Assesses the cognitive biases most likely to have shaped the assumption stack. Runs a default scan of six biases on every review: confirmation bias, overconfidence, planning fallacy, availability bias, sunk cost influence, and in-group bias. Expands contextually to include bandwagon effect, anchoring, optimism bias, and authority bias where the specific initiative warrants it. Each bias assessed as present is mapped to the specific assumptions it has most likely distorted.

**6. Minimum Viable Tests**
For each critical assumption with high or medium testability, designs the smallest, cheapest, fastest experiment that would meaningfully update confidence in that assumption. Each test specifies what it costs, what result would increase or decrease confidence, and what the founder would do differently based on each outcome. Tests without specified actions based on outcomes are research, not tests. The distinction matters.

**7. The Irreducible Bets**
Names the critical assumptions that cannot be tested before commitment. For each, establishes why it is low testability, what the downside looks like if it proves wrong, whether a structural hedge is available, and whether the founder is consciously accepting this bet or has not yet fully reckoned with it. Irreducible bets are not a reason to stop. They are the nature of strategic commitment. The goal is conscious acceptance rather than subsequent discovery.

**8. The Summary**
Presented before the full staged analysis. Uses first principles thinking to return to the root problem identified in Stage 2 and state whether the initiative addresses it directly or at a remove. Three sentences: the root problem or opportunity, the single most important finding from the review, and the recommended next action.

**9. Framing for Others**
Helps the founder communicate the assumption review to the relevant audience. Applies Toulmin's argumentation structure throughout: every claim accompanied by its grounds, the warrant connecting them, and acknowledgment of the conditions under which the claim would not hold. Three audience frames: co-founder or leadership team, board or investors, and personal record.

## How to use it with Claude

1. Open [Claude.ai](https://claude.ai)
2. Start a new conversation
3. Upload `SKILL.md` from this folder using the attachment icon
4. Tell Claude you want to review the assumptions behind an initiative. Claude will work through the stages with you, asking for what it needs at each step.

You do not need to prepare a structured brief in advance. The skill guides the conversation from the first prompt.

---

## As a standalone framework

The six assumption categories in Stage 3 serve as a checklist for any pre-commitment review without Claude. Work through each category, list every assumption, then apply the two-dimension classification from Stage 4. The assumptions that are both critical and testable form your immediate action list. The assumptions that are critical and untestable are the bets you are consciously accepting.

The bias list in Stage 5 applies equally well before a decision as after. Run it against your assumption set and ask which assumptions are most likely shaped by each bias rather than by evidence.
